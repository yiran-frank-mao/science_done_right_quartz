Clearly, the welding of two annuli remains an annulus topologically, we need to verify that the resulting annulus can be given a [[Complex Manifolds and Complex Structures#^98caa8|complex structure]]. In fact, since this is a local problem, it suffices to only consider about the welding of two disks. In other words, we shall verify that the welding of two disks is conformally equivalent to the Riemann sphere. However, such an equivalence mapping is not unique in general, so we need to fix a normalization condition to reduce the freedom. Suppose the two disks are $D_{-}$ and $D_{+}$, with $D_{-}$ containing $\infty$. Let $\newcommand{\Diff}{\mathrm{Diff}}f\in \Diff^+(S^1)$ be the welding map, then the unique conformal maps $\newcommand{\C}{\mathbb{C}}\Phi_{-}\colon D_{-}\to \hat{\C}\setminus \Phi_{+}(D_{+})$ and $\Phi_{+}\colon D_{+}\to \hat{\C}\setminus \Phi_{-}(D_{-})$ such that $$\Phi_{-}(z)=\Phi_{+}(f(z)) \quad \text{for all } z\in S^{1},$$and $\Phi_{-}$ satisfies the normalization condition $$\Phi_{-}(\infty)=\infty, \quad \Phi_{-}'(\infty)=1, \quad \Phi_{-}''(\infty)=0,$$are called the solution to the $f$-*welding problem*. The normalization condition can also be written out explicitly using series expansions: $$\Phi_{-}(z)=z + a_{-1} z^{-1} + a_{-2} z^{-2} + \cdots ,\quad\Phi_{+}(z)=a_{0}+a_{1}z+a_{2}z^{2}+\cdots.$$

> [!lemma] Consecutive Welding Lemma 
> An $\phi_{1}$-welding followed by a $\phi_{2}$-welding is equivalent to a single $\phi_{2}\circ \phi_{1}$-welding, for $\phi_{1},\phi_{2}\in \Diff^+(S^1)$. ^990688

*Proof*  The key point here is how to interpret the welding process. We consider the situation illustrated in the following diagram. Note that, rather than drawing the abstract Riemann surfaces, the spheres and disks shown are the images of the holomorphic embeddings of the welded Riemann surfaces into the Riemann sphere. Concretely, in the cut step we cut the $\phi_{1}$-welded Riemann surface along the seam, so that the corresponding image (the Riemann sphere) is cut along $\Phi^{(1)}(S^1)$. Performing a subsequent $\phi_{2}$-welding then requires a change of coordinates, since the seam is not the standard circle $S^1$.

So we have the following equations of the conformal welding problems: $$\begin{cases}\Phi^{(2)}_{-}(w)=\Phi_{+}^{(2)}(\bar{\phi_{2}}(w)) \quad \text{for all } w \in \Sigma,\\\Phi^{(1)}_{-}(z)=\Phi_{+}^{(1)}(\phi_{1}(z)) \quad \text{for all } z \in S^{1}.\end{cases} $$Now define $\Phi_{-}:= \Phi_{-}^{(2)}\circ \Phi_{-}^{(1)}$ and $\Phi_{+}:=\Phi_{+}^{(2)}\circ \Phi_{+}^{(1)}$, then for all $z\in S^{1}$ there holds $$\begin{aligned}\Phi_{-}(z)&=\Phi_{+}^{(2)}\left(\bar{\phi_{2}}\left(\Phi_{+}^{(1)}(\phi_{1}(z))\right)\right)\\&=\Phi_{+}^{(2)}\left(\Phi_{+}^{(1)}\circ \phi_{2}\circ (\Phi_{+}^{(1)})^{-1} \circ \Phi_{+}^{(1)}(\phi_{1}(z))\right)\\ &= \Phi_{+}^{(2)}\left(\Phi_{+}^{(1)}\circ \phi_{2}\circ \phi_{1}(z)\right)\\&= \Phi_{+}(\phi_{2}\circ \phi_{1} (z)).\end{aligned}$$Moreover, the normalization condition is met as well, say $\Phi_{-}^{(1)}(z)=z+a_{-1}z^{-1}+\cdots$ and $\Phi_{-}^{(2)}=z+b_{-1}z^{-1}+\cdots$, then $$\begin{aligned}\Phi_{-}(\infty)=\Phi_{-}^{(2)}\circ \Phi_{-}^{(1)}(\infty) = \Phi_{-}^{(2)}(\infty) =\infty,\\\Phi_{-}'(\infty)=(\Phi_{-}^{(2)}\circ \Phi_{-}^{(1)})'(\infty) = \Phi_{-}^{(2)\prime}(\Phi_{-}^{(1)}(\infty))\cdot \Phi_{-}^{(1)\prime}(\infty)=1,\\ \Phi_{-}''(\infty )= \Phi_{-}^{(2)\prime\prime}(\Phi_{-}^{(1)}(\infty))\left( \Phi_{-}^{(1)\prime}(\infty) \right)^{2} + \Phi_{-}^{(2)\prime}(\Phi_{-}^{(1)}(\infty)) \Phi_{-}^{(1)\prime\prime}(\infty)=0. \end{aligned}$$So $\Phi$ is the unique solution to the $\phi_{2}\circ \phi_{1}$-welding problem. $\square$

> [!lemma]
> Real analytic orientation preserving diffeomorphisms $\Diff_{\text{an}}^{+}(S^{1})$ are [[Closure, Interior and Boundary#^b560bf|dense]] in $\Diff^+(S^1)$ with respect to the $C^{1}$-topology.

*Proof*  For any $f\in \Diff^{+}(S^{1})$, we lift it to a smooth map $\newcommand{\R}{\mathbb{R}}\tilde{f}\colon \R\to \R$ such that $$\tilde{f}(x+2\pi)=\tilde{f}(x)+2\pi \text{ and } \tilde{f}'(x)>0 \text{ for all } x\in\R.$$Note that the derivative $\tilde{f}'$ is a $2\pi$-periodic smooth function, so by Fourier theory, we can approximate it uniformly by a trigonometric polynomial $p(x)$ which is everywhere positive. Define real analytic $$\newcommand{\dd}{\,\mathrm{d}}\tilde{f}_{\text{an}}(x):=\tilde{f}(0)+\int_{0}^{x}p(t) \dd t.$$If $p$ is chosen close enough to $\tilde{f}$ in sup-norm, then both $\tilde{f}_{\text{an}}$ and its derivative are uniformly close to $\tilde{f}$ and $\tilde{f}'$. This means $\| \tilde{f}-\tilde{f}_{\text{an}} \|_{C^{1}}$ can be made arbitrarily small, thus the real analytics are dense in $\Diff^{+}(S^{1})$. $\square$

> [!lemma]
> The $f$-welding problem (of two disks) has a unique solution for all $f\in \Diff^{+}(S^{1})$ that is close enough to the identity in $C^{1}$-topology.

*Proof*  Let $H^{2}(S^{1})\subset L^{2}(S^{1})$ be the Hardy space, $P$ be the projection onto the Hardy space, and $J=2P-I$ be the Hilbert transform. In other words, $$Jh(e^{i\theta})=\frac{1}{\pi i} \operatorname{p.v.} \int_{S^{1}} \frac{h(w)}{w-e^{i\theta}}\,\mathrm{d}w=\frac{1}{2\pi} \operatorname{p.v.} \int^{2\pi}_{0} \cot\left(\frac{\theta-\zeta}{2}\right) f(\zeta) \,\mathrm{d}\zeta.$$Notably, $J$ keeps the Hardy space unchanged and flips the orthogonal complement.
Let $V_{\phi}$ be the operator of precomposition by $\phi^{-1}\in \operatorname{Diff}^{+}(S^{1})$. Then the $\phi$-welding problem can be rephrased as follows: $$V_{\phi^{-1}}\Phi_{+}=\Phi_{-},\qquad J\Phi_{+}=\Phi_{+},\qquad J\Phi_{-}=2\iota-\Phi_{-},$$where $\iota\colon S^{1} \to \mathbb{C}$ is the inclusion map $\iota(e^{i\theta})=e^{i\theta}$. 
We first show that the operator $2I+J-V_{\phi^{-1}}JV_{\phi}$ is invertible, and if $\Phi_{-}$, $\Phi_{+}$ are a solution to the $\phi$-welding problem, then they must be of the form: $$\Phi_{-}=\left[2I+J-V_{\phi^{-1}}JV_{\phi}\right]^{-1}(2\iota),\quad \Phi_{+}=V_{\phi^{-1}}^{-1}\left[2I+J-V_{\phi^{-1}}JV_{\phi}\right]^{-1}(2\iota),$$i.e., the solution is unique. Notice that $$V_{\phi^{-1}}J V_{\phi} \Phi_{-}=V_{\phi^{-1}}J \Phi_{+} = V_{\phi^{-1}}\Phi_{+} = \Phi_{-},$$so $(J-V_{\phi^{-1}}JV_{\phi})\Phi_{-}=2\iota-2\Phi_{-}$, which yields $(2I+J-V_{\phi^{-1}}JV_{\phi})\Phi_{-}=2\iota$. Let us see how $V_{\phi^{-1}}JV_{\phi}$ acts on a function $f$: $$\begin{aligned} (V_{\phi^{-1}}JV_{\phi})(f)(z)&= (V_{\phi^{-1}}J)(f\circ \phi^{-1})(z) \\ &=J(f\circ \phi^{-1}) (\phi(z)) \\ &= \frac{1}{\pi i} \operatorname{p.v.} \int_{S^{1}} \frac{f\circ \phi^{-1}(w)}{w-\phi(z)}\,\mathrm{d}w \\ &= \frac{1}{\pi i} \operatorname{p.v.} \int_{S^{1}} \frac{f(\zeta)}{\phi(\zeta)-\phi(z)}\phi'(\zeta) \,\mathrm{d}\zeta, \end{aligned} \tag{1}$$where the derivative $\phi'(\zeta)$ should be understood as $$\phi'(\zeta) = \tilde{\phi}'(\arg \zeta) \frac{\phi(\zeta)}{\zeta}$$where $\tilde{\phi}$ is the smooth lift of $\phi$ so that $\phi(e^{it})=e^{i\tilde{\phi}(t)}$, and $\tilde{\phi}'(x)>0$ for all $x\in\mathbb{R}$.
Therefore, the integral kernel of $J-V_{\phi^{-1}}JV_{\phi}$ is $$K_{\phi}(z,\zeta)=\frac{1}{\zeta-z}-\frac{\phi'(\zeta)}{\phi(\zeta)-\phi(z)}.$$In angular coordinates, writing $\zeta=e^{i\eta}$ and $z=e^{i\theta}$, we obtain$$K_{\phi}(z,\zeta)=\frac{1}{e^{i\eta}-e^{i\theta}}-\frac{1}{e^{i\eta}}\frac{\tilde{\phi}'(\eta)e^{i\tilde{\phi}(\eta)}}{e^{i\tilde{\phi}(\eta)}-e^{i\tilde{\phi}(\theta)}}.$$To prove that $K_\phi$ is smooth, we first show that $$\tilde{K}_{\phi}(\eta,\theta) = \frac{1}{\eta-\theta} - \frac{\tilde{\phi}'(\eta)}{\tilde{\phi}(\eta)-\tilde{\phi}(\theta)}$$extends smoothly to $\mathbb{R}^2$. By Hadamard's lemma (Nestruev, 2020, Lemma 2.8), we may write $$\tilde{\phi}(\eta)-\tilde{\phi}(\theta)=(\eta-\theta) \tilde{q} (\eta,\theta) \tag{2}$$for some smooth function $\tilde{q}$. Explicitly, $$\tilde{q}(\eta,\theta)=\int_{0}^{1}\tilde{\phi}'(\theta+t(\eta-\theta)) \,\mathrm{d}t.$$Differentiating Equation (2) with respect to $\eta$ gives $$\tilde{\phi}'(\eta) = \tilde{q}(\eta,\theta) + (\eta-\theta) \frac{\partial \tilde{q}}{\partial \eta}(\eta,\theta).$$Hence, $$\begin{aligned} \tilde{K}_{\phi}(\eta,\theta) &= \frac{1}{\eta-\theta} - \frac{\tilde{\phi}'(\eta)}{\tilde{\phi}(\eta)-\tilde{\phi}(\theta)} \\ &= \frac{1}{\eta-\theta} - \frac{\tilde{q}(\eta,\theta) + (\eta-\theta) \frac{\partial \tilde{q}}{\partial \eta}(\eta,\theta)}{(\eta-\theta) \tilde{q} (\eta,\theta)} \\ &= \frac{1}{\eta-\theta} - \frac{1}{\eta-\theta} - \frac{1}{\tilde{q} (\eta,\theta)}\frac{\partial \tilde{q}}{\partial \eta}(\eta,\theta) \\ &= - \frac{1}{\tilde{q} (\eta,\theta)}\frac{\partial \tilde{q}}{\partial \eta}(\eta,\theta). \end{aligned}$$Thus, $\tilde{K}_{\phi}$ is smooth.
The same argument applies to $K_\phi$. Indeed, we may rewrite $$K_{\phi}(z,\zeta) = \frac{1}{ie^{i\eta}} \left( \frac{ie^{i\eta}}{e^{i\eta}-e^{i\theta}} -\frac{i\tilde{\phi}'(\eta)e^{i\tilde{\phi}(\eta)}}{e^{i\tilde{\phi}(\eta)} - e^{i\tilde{\phi}(\theta)}}\right).$$By applying the preceding argument to the functions $x\mapsto e^{ix}$ and $x\mapsto e^{i\tilde{\phi}(x)}$, the two singular terms reduce to $$\begin{aligned} \frac{ie^{i\eta}}{e^{i\eta}-e^{i\theta}} &= \frac{1}{\eta-\theta}+\frac{1}{q(\eta,\theta)}\frac{\partial q(\eta,\theta)}{\partial \eta}, \\ \frac{i\tilde{\phi}'(\eta)e^{i\tilde{\phi}(\eta)}}{e^{i\tilde{\phi}(\eta)} - e^{i\tilde{\phi}(\theta)}} &= \frac{\tilde{\phi}'(\eta)}{\tilde{\phi}(\eta)-\tilde{\phi}(\theta)} + \frac{1}{q(\tilde{\phi}(\eta),\tilde{\phi}(\theta))}\frac{\partial q(\tilde{\phi}(\eta),\tilde{\phi}(\theta))}{\partial \eta}. \end{aligned}$$Consequently, the singular parts cancel as in the case of $\tilde{K}_{\phi}$, and $K_\phi$ is smooth. In particular, the principal value is no longer needed: $$(J-V_{\phi^{-1}}JV_{\phi}) f(z) = \frac{1}{\pi i} \int_{S^1} K_{\phi}(z,\zeta)f(\zeta) \,\mathrm{d}\zeta.$$Since $S^1\times S^1$ is compact and $K_\phi$ is smooth, $J-V_{\phi^{-1}}JV_{\phi}$ is Hilbert-Schmidt, with Hilbert-Schmidt norm $$\|J-V_{\phi^{-1}}JV_{\phi}\|^2_{\mathrm{HS}} = \iint_{S^1\times S^1} |K_{\phi}(z,\zeta)|^2 \,\mathrm{d}z \,\mathrm{d}\zeta.$$Note that assuming $\phi\to\mathrm{id}$ in $C^2$ (equivalently $\tilde{\phi} \to \mathrm{id}$ in $C^2$), then from the displayed formula above, $\tilde{q},q\to 1$ in at least $C^1$. So $K_\phi(z,\zeta)\to 0$ uniformly. Consequently, $$\|J-V_{\phi^{-1}}JV_{\phi}\|_{\mathrm{HS}} \to 0,$$and for $\phi$ sufficiently $C^2$-close to the identity, we have $$\|J-V_{\phi^{-1}}JV_{\phi}\|_{\mathrm{op}} \leq \|J-V_{\phi^{-1}}JV_{\phi}\|_{\mathrm{HS}} < 2.$$Therefore, $$2I+J-V_{\phi^{-1}}JV_{\phi} = 2 \left(I+\frac{1}{2} (J-V_{\phi^{-1}}JV_{\phi}) \right)$$is invertible by the [[Normed and Banach Algebras#^9e9db9|Neumann series]].
It remains to verify that such $\Phi_{-}$ and $\Phi_{+}$ constitute a solution to the $\phi$-welding problem. When $\phi$ is real analytic, $\phi$ extends holomorphically to annuli on both sides of $S^{1}$, so the welded surface $D_{-}\sqcup_{\phi}D_{+}$ is conformally equivalent to the Riemann sphere by the [[Riemann Mapping Theorem#^558348|Riemann uniformisation theorem]]. For arbitrary $\phi\in \operatorname{Diff}^{+}(S^{1})$, since $\left[2I+J-V_{\phi^{-1}}JV_{\phi}\right]^{-1}$ depends continuously on $\phi$, and $\operatorname{Diff}_{\text{an}}^{+}(S^{1})\subset \operatorname{Diff}^{+}(S^{1})$ is dense, $\left[2I+J-V_{\phi^{-1}}JV_{\phi}\right]^{-1}(2\iota)$ is a solution to the $\phi$-welding problem for arbitrary $\phi$ as well. $\square$

> [!theorem]
> The $f$-welding problem (of two disks) has a unique solution for all $f\in \Diff^{+}(S^{1})$, thus the welding operation is a well-defined semigroup operation on $\newcommand{\Ann}{\mathrm{Ann}}\Ann$. ^280fe6

*Proof*  From the above lemma, we know that every $f$-welding problem has a unique solution for $f$ close to the identity. Note that $\Diff^{+}(S^{1})$ is generated by any neighborhood of the identity, so [[Conformal Welding Problem#^990688|the lemma]] implies that the welding operation is well-defined for all $f\in \Diff^{+}(S^{1})$. It remains to check that the welding is independent of choice of representative. Suppose we have two equivalent annuli $\newcommand{\phiin}{\gamma_{\text{in}}}\newcommand{\phiout}{\gamma_{\text{out}}}(\Sigma, \phiin, \phiout)$ and $(\Sigma', \phiin', \phiout')$ with biholomorphic map $f\colon \Sigma\to \Sigma'$. When welding $(\Sigma, \phiin, \phiout)$ with another annulus $(\tilde{\Sigma}, \tilde{\varphi}_{\text{in}}, \tilde{\varphi}_{\text{out}})$, we can define $F\colon \Sigma\cdot \tilde{\Sigma} \to \Sigma'\cdot \tilde{\Sigma}$ by $$F(x)=\begin{cases} f(x), & x\in \Sigma,\\ x, & x\in \tilde{\Sigma}.\end{cases}$$This map is well-defined and biholomorphic, so the two welded annuli are equivalent. $\square$
