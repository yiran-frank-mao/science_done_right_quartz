## Embed $\Diff^+(S^1)$ into $\RSP(\H)$

Consider the real vector space $C^{\infty}(S^{1},\R)/\R$ of smooth real-valued functions on the circle modulo constants (i.e. mean-zero functions on the ), equipped with the (real) inner product $$\newcommand{\pv}{{\small{\operatorname{p.v.}}}}\newcommand{\dd}{\,\mathrm{d}} \langle f,g\rangle=\frac{1}{4\pi^{2}} \int_{0}^{2\pi} \pv \int^{2\pi}_{0} \cot\left(\frac{\theta-\zeta}{2}\right) f(\theta) g'(\zeta) \dd\theta \dd\zeta = \sum_{n\neq 0} |n|f_{n} g_{-n}.$$Let $\H_{\R}=\overline{C^{\infty}(S^{1},\R)/\R}$ be the completion of $C^{\infty}(S^{1},\R)/\R$ with respect to the above inner product. The Hilbert transform $$\newcommand{\sgn}{\operatorname{sgn}}(J f)(\theta) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int^{2\pi}_{0} \cot\left(\frac{\theta-\zeta}{2}\right) f(\zeta) \dd\zeta=\sum_{n\neq 0} i\sgn(n) f_{n}e^{in \theta}$$forms a complex structure on $\H_{\R}$. Now, let us apply the procedure described at the beginning of this section to construct a symplectic Hilbert space $\H$ from $\H_{\R}$. Then the underlying set of $\H$ is $\overline{C^{\infty}(S^{1})/\C}$, the orthogonal subspaces $\H^{+}$ and $\H^{-}$ consists of functions that can extend holomorphically to $D_{+}$ and $D_{-}$ respectively. The symplectic form is given by $$\omega(f,g)=\langle f, g\rangle = i\sum_{n\neq 0} n f_{-n}g_{n} = \frac{1}{2\pi} \oint_{S^{1}} f(z)\dd g(z).$$Note that $i\sum_{n\neq 0} n f_{-n}g_{n}$ is real for real functions $f$ and $g$.

> [!proposition]
> Suppose $\phi\in\Diff^{+}(S^{1})$, then the operator $V_{\phi}\colon f \mapsto f\circ \phi^{-1} \in \RSP(\H)$.  
> 

*Proof*  $V_{\phi}$ is symplectic, because $$\omega(V_{\phi} f, V_{\phi} g)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\oint_{S^{1}} f(\phi(z))\dd g(\phi(z))=\omega(f,g).$$To show that $V_{\phi}$ is in $\RSP(\H)$, it suffices to show that $V_{\phi}^{*}V_{\phi}$ can be written as a Hilbert-Schmidt perturbation of the identity. Once we can write $\newcommand{\HS}{\text{HS}}V_{\phi}^{*}V_{\phi}=1+T_{\HS}$, we can apply the polar decomposition and functional calculus to write $V_{\phi}=U(1+T_{\HS})$ for some unitary $U$.

## Embed $\overline{\Ann}$ into $\CSP(\H)$

The embedding $V\colon \Diff^{+}(S^{1})\hookrightarrow \RSP(\H)$ is natural in the sense that the change of variable is the only thing we can do given a diffeomorphism. Similarly, if we want to treat an annulus as an operator on $\H$, the natural way is to holomorphically "extend" some function from the one boundary to the other boundary, and then restrict it back to the circle. The following proposition shows this idea precisely.

> [!proposition] 
> There is a well-defined [[Relations and Functions#^042daf|injective]] semigroup homomorphism $\overline{\Ann} \hookrightarrow \CSP(\H)$ extending $V$ and sending an annulus $\Sigma=[(S,\Phi^{-},\Phi^{+})]$ to the closure of an (unbounded) operator $V_{\Sigma}$ on $\H$ satisfying $$ h\bigg|_{\partial_{-}\Sigma} \circ \Phi^{-}\bigg|_{S^{1}}= f, \quad h\bigg|_{\partial_{+}\Sigma}\circ \Phi^{+}\bigg|_{S^{1}}= \Gamma_{\Sigma}(f) $$for some holomorphic $h\in H(\Sigma)$.

We shall now check that $V_{\Sigma}$ always lands in $\CSP(\H)$. Let us pick the standard representative $\newcommand{\bgamma}{\bar{\gamma}}(A_{t}, \bgamma_{-}, \bgamma_{+})$ of an annulus $\Sigma$, and consider the trivial round annulus $\newcommand{\id}{1}(A_{t}, e^{-t}\id_{\Diff^{+}(S^1)},\id_{\Diff^{+}(S^1)})$ that is of the same modulus. Denote $\Sigma_{0}=[(A_{t}, e^{-t},\id_{\Diff^{+}(S^1)})]\in\overline{\Ann}$. For $f,g\in\H$, $g=V_{\Sigma}(f)$ means there exists $h\in H(\Sigma)$ such that $$h\bigg|_{\partial_{-}A_{t}}\circ \bgamma_{-}=f,\quad h\bigg|_{\partial_{+}A_{t}} \circ \bgamma_{+}=g.$$These are exactly $$h\bigg|_{\partial_{-}A_{t}} = f \circ \bgamma_{-}^{-1} = V_{\bgamma_{-}^{-1}} f, \quad h\bigg|_{\partial_{+}A_{t}} \circ e^{-t}\id_{\Diff^{+}(S^1)}=g \circ (e^{t} \bgamma_{+})^{-1}= V_{(e^{t} \bgamma_{+})^{-1}}g,$$which are precisely conditions for $V_{(e^{t}\bgamma_{+})^{-1}} g = V_{\Sigma_{0}} (V_{\bgamma_{-}^{-1}} f)$. Therefore,  $$g=V_{\Sigma}(f) \iff V_{(e^{t}\gamma_{+})^{-1}}g = V_{\Sigma_{0}} (V_{\gamma_{-}^{-1}}f).$$This further means $$V_{\Sigma} = V_{e^{t}\bgamma_{+}} V_{\Sigma_{0}} V_{\bgamma_{-}^{-1}}.$$Note that we have already showed that $V$ embeds diffeomorphisms to $\RSP(\H)$, so it remains to show that $V_{\Sigma_{0}}$ lands in $\CSP(\H)$. Suppose $g=V_{\Sigma_{0}} f$, we have the following equations hold: $$h\bigg|_{\partial_{-}A_{t}} = f,\quad h\bigg|_{\partial_{+}A_{t}} \circ e^{-t}\id_{\Diff^{+}(S^1)} = g.$$Since $h$ is holomorphic on $A_{t}$, we shall write $h$ in Laurent series: $h(z)=\sum_{n} h_{n} z^{n}$. We can also write $f$ in Fourier series $f(e^{i\theta})=\sum_{n \neq 0} f_{n} e^{in\theta}$, hence the first equation implies $f_{n}=h_{n}$, and the second equation implies $g_{n}=e^{-tn}h_{n}$, so $$V_{\Sigma_{0}}\colon \sum_{n \neq 0} f_{n} e^{in\theta} \mapsto \sum_{n \neq 0} e^{-tn} f_{n} e^{in\theta}.$$Therefore, $V_{\Sigma_{0}}$ satisfies $$\begin{pmatrix} f_{-} \\ (V_{\Sigma_{0}}f)_{+} \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix} 0 & K_{t} \\ K_{t} & 0\end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} f_{+} \\ (Tf)_{-} \end{pmatrix},$$where $K_{t}\colon \sum_{n \neq 0} f_{n} e^{in\theta} \mapsto \sum_{n \neq 0} e^{-t|n|} f_{n} e^{in\theta}$ is a contraction. Now it is clear $V_{\Sigma_{0}}\in \CSP(\H)$.
Next we show that $V$ is multiplicative. Let $\Sigma_{1}=(S_{1}, \Phi_{1}^{-}, \Phi_{1}^{+})$ and $\Sigma_{2}=(S_{2}, \Phi_{2}^{-}, \Phi_{2}^{+})$ be two annuli, and $\Sigma_{3}=\Sigma_{2}\cdot \Sigma_{1}$ be the welded annulus. We want to show that $V_{\Sigma_{3}}=V_{\Sigma_{2}}V_{\Sigma_{1}}$. Let $f\in\H$, then $g=V_{\Sigma_{1}} f$ means there exists $h\in H(\Sigma_{1})$ such that $$h\bigg|_{\partial_{-}\Sigma_{1}} \circ \Phi^{-}_{1}\bigg|_{S^{1}}=f,\quad h\bigg|_{\partial_{+}\Sigma_{1}}\circ \Phi^{+}_{1}\bigg|_{S^{1}}=g.$$Similarly, $k=V_{\Sigma_{2}} g$ means there exists $h'\in H(\Sigma_{2})$ such that $$h'\bigg|_{\partial_{-}\Sigma_{2}}\circ \Phi^{-}_{2}\bigg|_{S^{1}}=g,\quad h'\bigg|_{\partial_{+}\Sigma_{2}}\circ \Phi^{+}_{2}\bigg|_{S^{1}}=k.$$Since $\partial_{+}\Sigma_{1}$ is identified with $\partial_{-}\Sigma_{2}$ in the welding, we can glue $h$ and $h'$ together to get a holomorphic function on $\Sigma_{3}$, which shows that $V_{\Sigma_3} f = k = V_{\Sigma_2} g = V_{\Sigma_2} V_{\Sigma_1} f$. $\square$
