## Quantum Numbers

>[!definition] Quantum Numbers
>The four quantum numbers $n$, $l$, $m$, and $s$ specify the complete and unique quantum state of a single electron in an atom:
>- $n$: principal quantum number;
>- $l$: orbital quantum number, azimuthal quantum number;
>- $m$: magnetic quantum number;
>- $s$: spin quantum number.

## Orbital Angular Momentum

**Def**  <i><u>Orbital Angular Momentum Operator</u></i>
Orbital angular momentum operators are: $$\begin{aligned} \hat{L}_{x}=\hat{y}\hat{p}_{z}-\hat{z}\hat{p}_{y}=-i\hbar\left(y\frac{\partial}{\partial z}-z\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\right) \\ \hat{L}_{y}=\hat{z}\hat{p}_{x}-\hat{x}\hat{p}_{z}=-i\hbar\left(z\frac{\partial}{\partial x}-x\frac{\partial}{\partial z}\right)\\ \hat{L}_{z}=\hat{x}\hat{p}_{y}-\hat{y}\hat{p}_{x}=-i\hbar\left(x\frac{\partial}{\partial y}-y\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\right) \end{aligned}$$Or in spherical polar coordinates: $$\begin{aligned}
\hat{L}_{x}&=i\hbar\left(\sin\phi \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}+\cot\theta\cos\phi\frac{\partial}{\partial\phi}\right) \\ 
\hat{L}_{y}&=i\hbar \left(-\cos \phi \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}+\cot \theta\sin\phi\frac{\partial}{\partial\phi}\right)\\
\hat{L}_{z}&=-i\hbar\frac{\partial}{\partial\phi}
\end{aligned}$$In matrix representation under condition $l=1$: $$\hat{L}_{x}=\begin{pmatrix}0&\frac{\hbar}{\sqrt{2}}&0\\\frac{\hbar}{\sqrt{2}}&0&\frac{\hbar}{\sqrt{2}}\\0&\frac{\hbar}{\sqrt{2}}&0\end{pmatrix} ,\quad
\hat{L}_{y}=\begin{pmatrix}0&\frac{i\hbar}{\sqrt{2}}&0\\\frac{i\hbar}{\sqrt{2}}&0&\frac{i\hbar}{\sqrt{2}}\\0&\frac{i\hbar}{\sqrt{2}}&0\end{pmatrix} ,\quad \hat{L}_z=\begin{pmatrix}-\hbar&0&0\\0&0&0\\0&0&\hbar\end{pmatrix}$$

>[!proposition] 
>**Prop**  The angular momentum commutator is given by$$\begin{aligned}[]
>[\hat{L}_{x}, \hat{L}_{y}]=i \hbar L_{z} \\
>[\hat{L}_{y}, \hat{L}_{z}]=i \hbar L_{x} \\
>[\hat{L}_{z}, \hat{L}_{x}]=i \hbar L_{y}
>\end{aligned}$$Thus the components of angular momentum are not [[Uncertainty#^60a9fd|compatible]].

>[!remark]
>This means we cannot make simultaneous measurements of the components of the angular momentum with arbitrary precision.

>[!proposition] 
>**Prop**  The commutator between a particular component of angular momentum and total angular momentum squared is zero: $$[\hat{L}_{x}, \hat{L}^{2}]=0$$

**Lemma**  In a central potential, we have the following holds:$$[\hat{L}^{2},\hat{H}]=0$$Thus $\hat{L}^{2}$ and $\hat{H}$ share simultaneous eigenfunctions.

## Ladder Operators

**Def**  <i><u>Orbital Ladder Operators</u></i>
Define the ladder operators as the following: $$\hat{L}_{+}=\hat{L}_{x}+i\hat{L}_{y},\quad \hat{L}_{-}=\hat{L}_{x}-i\hat{L}_y$$These are called the raising and lowering operators respectively.

**Prop**  Ladder operators commutes with $\hat{L}^{2}$.

**Prop**  Ladder operators act on the wavefunction to produce new wavefunctions which are also eigenstates of $\hat{L}^{2}$ belonging to the eigenvalue but which have higher or lower $m_{l}$.

**Thrm**  $$\begin{aligned} \hat{L}_{+}\ket{\lambda,m_{l}}=\hbar\sqrt{\lambda-m_{l}(m_{l}+1)}\ket{\lambda,m_{l}+1} \\ \hat{L}_-\ket{\lambda,m_{l}}=\hbar\sqrt{\lambda-m_l(m_l-1)}\ket{\lambda,m_{l}-1} \end{aligned} $$
**Thrm**  Suppose $m_{l}\hbar$ is the eigenvalue associated with $\hat{L}_{z}$ and $\lambda \hbar^{2}$ is the eigenvalue associated with $\hat{L}^{2}$, then we have the following relation holds: $$m_{l}=l, l-1, l-2, \dots -l,\quad \lambda = l(l+1)$$And we usually label states of definite angular momentum with $l$ and $m_{l}$: $\ket{l,m_{l}}$.
<u><b>e.g.</b></u>  $\ket{2, 0}$.

**Corollary**  The number of states with different observables for $\hat{L}_{z}$ are $2l+1$.

**Prop**  We have following operator actions: $$\begin{aligned}&\hat{L}^{2}|l,m_{l}\rangle=l(l+1)\hbar^{2}|l,m_{l}\rangle\\&\hat{L}_{z}|l,m_{l}\rangle=m_{l}\hbar|l,m_{l}\rangle\\ &\hat{L}_{+}|l,m_{l}\rangle=\hbar\sqrt{l(l+1)-m_{l}(m_{l}+1)}|l,m_{l}+1\rangle\\&\hat{L}_{-}|l,m_{l}\rangle=\hbar\sqrt{l(l+1)-m_{l}(m_{l}-1)}|l,m_{l}-1\rangle\end{aligned}$$

**Def**  <i><u>Spherical Harmonics</u></i>
We denote $Y_{l}^{m}(\theta, \phi)$ as the eigenfunction of $\hat{L}^{2}$, called spherical harmonics. And thus we have the following formula: $$\hat{L}^{2}Y_{l}^{m}(\theta, \phi)=l(l+1)\hbar^{2}Y_{l}^{m}(\theta, \phi)$$The solutions to the spherical harmonics are given by: $$Y_l^{-l}(\theta,\phi)=\frac1{2^ll}\sqrt{\frac{(2l+1)!}{4\pi}}e^{-il\phi}(\sin\theta)^{l}$$which is the eigenfunction with the lowest value of $m$. All other eigenfunctions can then be generated by successive application of the ladder operator $\hat{L}_{+}$. Table of spherical harmonics can be found [here](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Table_of_spherical_harmonics).
