> [!definition] Cyclic Group
> A group $G$ is cyclic if
> $$ G=\langle a\rangle=\left\{\ldots, a^{-3}, a^{-2}, a^{-1}, e, a, a^2, a^3, \ldots\right\} $$
> that is, every $g \in G$ has the form $g = a^n$ for some $n \in \Z$. We say that $G$ is generated by $a$.

<b><u>e.g.</u></b>  $\Z$ is cyclic, generated by either 1 or −1.

**Prop** Let $G = ⟨a⟩$ be a cyclic group. Then exactly one of the following holds:
1. $G$ is infinite
2. There exists $n\in\N$ such that $G =\{e,a,a^2,...,a^{n−1}\}$.
3

**Proof**  If $G$ is finite, To prove exists $n\in\N$ such that $G =\{e,a,a^2,...,a^{n−1}\}$ is equivalent to prove that for all $k\in \Z$, exists $n<k$ and $r<n$ such that $a^k=a^r$. By the pigeonhole principle, for any integer $l$ exists $m$ that $m<l$ we have $a^l=a^m$. Hence $a^{l-m}=e$, so $a$ has a finite order $o(a)$. Let $n=o(a)$. Then for every $k \in \Z$, $k=qn+r$ with $q\in\Z$ and $0\leq r<n$. It follows that
$$ a^k=a^{n q+r}=\left(a^n\right)^q a^r=e^q a^r=a^r $$

> [!proposition]
> If a group is cyclic, then it is abelian.

**Prop** Suppose $G$ is a group. If there exists $g\in G$ such that $o(g)=|G|$ then $G$ is cyclic.

**Prop**  Any group with prime order is cyclic.

> [!definition]
> A product of two finite cyclic groups with coprime orders is cyclic. i.e. 

> [!theorem] The Structure Theorem for Finite Abelian Groups
> Every finite abelian group $G$ is isomorphic to a direct product (sum) of cyclic groups: $$G\cong C_{1}\times C_{2}\times\cdots\times C_{n},$$such that $|C_{i}|$ divides $|C_{i+1}|$ for all $i\geq1$, and $|G|=|C_{1}|\cdots|C_{n}|$.
