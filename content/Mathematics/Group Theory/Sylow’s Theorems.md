Sylow's theorems are direct applications of group actions and form a generalisation of Cauchy's theorem.

## The Existence Theorem

> [!theorem] First Sylow's Theorem
> Let $G$ be a [[Groups, Order and Subgroups#^6e0960|group]] of [[Groups, Order and Subgroups#^3bce31|order]] $p^{n}m$, where $p$ is a [[Division and Prime#^47f235|prime]] and $p\nmid m$. Then $G$ has a [[Groups, Order and Subgroups#^1ccb07|subgroup]] of order $p^{n}$, we call it *$p$-Sylow subgroup*. ^6d90f2

*Proof*  Without loss of generality, we can assume that $n\geq 1$. We use induction on $|G |$. Clearly for $|G | = 1$ there is nothing to prove. Assume it is true for all groups $H$ with order smaller than $p^{n}m$. Suppose it is false for $G$, that is, $G$ has no subgroups of order $p^{n}$. Then, by our induction hypothesis, for every proper subgroup $H < G$, $p^{n}\nmid |H|$. In particular, if $a\notin Z(G)$, then its [[Abelianisation of Groups#^6d802d|centraliser]] $C_{G}(a)$ is a proper subgroup of $G$, and hence $p^{n}\nmid |C_{G}(a)|$. By [[Group Actions#^becec5|class equation]], we have $$ p^{n}m= |G|=|Z(G)|+\sum_{i}[G:C_G(x_i)] $$where $x_i$ are representatives of the non-trivial conjugacy classes. Since $p^{n}\mid |G|$ and $p^{n}\nmid |C_{G}(x_i)|$, we must have $p \mid |Z(G)|$. By [[Group Actions#^ff735e|Cauchy's theorem]], there exists an element $z\in Z(G)$ of order $p$. Let $\langle z\rangle$ be the subgroup generated by $z$. Then $\langle z\rangle\triangleleft G$ and consider the quotient group $G/\langle z\rangle$. We have $$ |G/\langle z\rangle|=\frac{|G|}{|\langle z\rangle|}=\frac{p^{n}m}{p}=p^{n-1}m. $$By our induction hypothesis, there exists a subgroup of order $p^{n-1}$ in $G/\langle z\rangle$, say $K$. Let $\pi\colon G\to G/\langle z\rangle$ be the quotient map. Then $\pi^{-1}(K)$ is a subgroup of order $p^{n}$ in $G$ (ref. [[Isomorphism Theorems#^0f045a|correspondence theorem]]), which is a contradiction. Therefore, our assumption that $G$ has no subgroups of order $p^{n}$ is false. $\square$

<u><b>e.g.</b></u>  
- Consider the [[Permutations and Symmetric Groups#^5b562f|symmetric group]] $S_{4}$, whose [[Groups, Order and Subgroups#^3bce31|order]] is $24=2^{3}\cdot 3$. [[Sylowâ€™s Theorems#^6d90f2|Sylow's I]] states that $S_{4}$ has a subgroup of order $2^{3}=8$. Indeed, the subgroup $\langle (1 2),(34),(1324)\rangle$ is a subgroup of order $8$. 
- For the [[Dihedral Groups#^0206e0|dihedral group]] $D_{10}$ of order $10=2\cdot 5$, [[Sylowâ€™s Theorems#^6d90f2|Sylow's I]] states that $D_{10}$ has a subgroup of order $5$. Indeed, the subgroup $\langle r\rangle$ generated by a rotation is a subgroup of order $5$.
$\quad$

## The Classification Theorem

> [!lemma]
> Suppose $X$ is a finite set and $G$ is a $p$-group acting on $X$, and $p$ does not divide $|X|$, then the action must have a fixed point.  ^a6b4e3

*Proof*  Every orbit size is a power of $p$ (ref.  [[Group Actions#^dac142|Orbit-stabliser theorem]]), so each orbit has size either $1$ or a power of $p$, and a sum of such numbers can be non-multiple of $p$ only if at least one orbit has size $1$. $\square$

> [!theorem] Second Sylow's Theorem
> Let $G$ be a finite group and $p$ a [[Division and Prime#^47f235|prime]] dividing $|G|$. Then
> - Any two $p$-Sylow subgroups in a finite group $G$ are conjugate;
> - Any $p$-subgroup of $G$ is contained in a $p$-Sylow subgroup.
> $\quad$ ^f2aeb1

*Proof*  Let $H$ be a $p$-subgroup, and $P$ be a $p$-Sylow subgroup. We will show that there is $g\in G$ such that $gHg^{-1}\leq P$. Once we have that, both parts of the theorem are proved. Consider the (left multiplication) action of $G$ on the [[Cosets and Lagrangeâ€™s Theorem#^b825de|left coset space]] $G/P$. While this is [[Group Actions#^065d30|transitive]], the (sub) action of $H$ must have a fixed point by [[Sylowâ€™s Theorems#^a6b4e3|the above lemma]]. Let $gP\in G/P$ be a fixed point of the action of $H$, then for all $h\in H$, we have $hgP=gP$, which implies that $g^{-1}hg\in P$. Therefore, $gHg^{-1}\leq P$. $\square$

> [!remark]+
> The converse of the first part of Sylow II also holds. That is, If $H$ is a $p$-Sylow subgroup, $gHg^{âˆ’1}$ is also a $p$-Sylow subgroup, because conjugating by an element evidently produces a subgroup of the same size.
> 

<u><b>e.g.</b></u>  For $D_{10}$, every subgroup of size $2$ is generated by a refection, and Sylow II indicates that all the refections are conjugate.

## The Counting Theorem

> [!theorem] Third Sylowâ€™s Theorem
> The number of [[Sylowâ€™s Theorems#^6d90f2|$p$-Sylow subgroups]] in a finite group $G$ of order $p^{n}m$ with $p\nmid m$ is of the form $1+kp$ and divides $m$. ^73ca7e

*Proof*  Let $X=\{Q \mid  Q \text{ is a }p\text{-Sylow subgroup of }G\}$. Consider the conjugation action of $G$ on $X$. By [[Sylowâ€™s Theorems#^f2aeb1|Sylow II]], we know all $p$-Sylow subgroups are conjugate, so the action is transitive, hence by [[Group Actions#^dac142|orbit-stabiliser theorem]] $X$ is [[Relations and Functions#^042daf|bijective]] to $G/G_{P}$ for all $P\in X$. Note that the stabliser $G_{P}$ is exactly the [[Homomorphisms, Normal Subgroup & Conjugation#^d4bf2b|normaliser]] $N_{G}(P)$. So $P\leq G_{P} \leq G$ and therefore $|X|=[G:G_{P}]$ divides $[G:P]=m$. 
Now, restrict the action from $G$ to $P$. We claim that the action of $P$ on $X$ has exactly one fixed point, namely $P$. Firstly, since $|X|$ divides $m$, $p \nmid |X|$. So the fixed point exists because of the [[Sylowâ€™s Theorems#^a6b4e3|lemma]]. Secondly, we show that $P$ is the only fixed point. Suppose $Q\in X$ is some $p$-Sylow subgroup that is fixed by $P$, that is, $aQ a^{-1}=Q$ for all $a\in P$. Then $P \leq N_{G}(Q)$. Thus, in $G_{Q}=N_{G}(Q)$, we have two $p$-Sylow subgroups, $P$ and $Q$. By [[Sylowâ€™s Theorems#^f2aeb1|Sylow II]], they must be conjugate in $G_{Q}$, but $Q$ is normal in $N_{G}(Q)$, so $P=Q$. Therefore, $P$ is the only fixed point of the action of $P$ on $X$. By [[Group Actions#^becec5|class equation]], we have $$ |X|=1+kp $$for some integer $k$. $\square$

<u><b>e.g.</b></u>  There is exactly one $7$-Sylow subgroup in a group of order $35$ and it is normal. More generally, If $|G| = pq$ with primes $p > q$, then the $p$-Sylow subgroup in $G$ is normal because the only integer of the form $1 + k p$ that divides $q$ is $1$.

## Examples of Sylow's Theorems

<u><b>e.g.</b></u>  We can use Sylow's theorems to show that any group $G$ of order $15$ is isomorphic to $C_{5}\times C_{3}$. Note that $15=5\cdot 3$, so by [[Sylowâ€™s Theorems#^73ca7e|Sylow III]], there is a unique $5$-Sylow subgroup $H\triangleright G$. Similarly, there is a unique $3$-Sylow subgroup $K\triangleright G$. Note that nontrivial elements of $H$ have order $5$, while elements of $K$ have order $3$, so they intersect only at the identity, i.e., $H\cap K=\{e\}$. Therefore, $G\cong H\times K\cong C_{5}\times C_{3}$ by the [[Homomorphisms, Normal Subgroup & Conjugation#^579a54|proposition]].
