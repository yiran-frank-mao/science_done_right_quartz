## Field Extensions and Common Fields

> [!definition] Field Extension
> The field $K$ is an extension of the field $F$ if $F\subset K$. We write $K/F$ to denote this extension.

Here are the three most important classes of fields:

> [!definition] Number Fields, Finite Fields and Function Fields
> A number field is a subfield of $\newcommand{\C}{\mathbb{C}}\C$. 
> A finite field is a field with finitely many elements.
> A function field is an extension field of $\C(t)$ of rational functions in one variable.
> 

## Algebraic and Transcendental Elements

> [!definition] Algebraic & Transcendental
> Suppose $K\supset F$ is an extension of a field $F$. An element $\alpha\in K$ is *algebraic* over $F$ if there exists a nonzero monic polynomial $f\in F[x]$ such that $f(\alpha)=0$. Otherwise, it is called *transcendental*.

<u><b>e.g.</b></u>  $2\pi i$ is algebraic over $\newcommand{\R}{\mathbb{R}}\R$ but transcendental over $\newcommand{\Q}{\mathbb{Q}}\Q$.

> [!proposition]
> Suppose $K$ is an extension of a field $F$. Fix some $\alpha\in K$, consider $$\varphi\colon F[x]\to K, \quad x\mapsto \alpha.$$Then $\alpha$ is algebraic over $F$ if and only if $\ker\varphi\neq 0$. Equivalently, $\alpha$ is transcendental over $F$ if and only if $\varphi$ is injective.
> 

*Proof*  This is a direct consequence of the definition. $\square$

> [!proposition]
> Fix some algebraic $\alpha\in K$ over $F$. Then the following are equivalent for a monic polynomial $f\in F[x]$:
> - $f$ is the monic polynomial of lowest degree such that $f(\alpha)=0$.
> - $f$ is irreducible in $F[x]$ and $f(\alpha)=0$.
> - $f(\alpha)=0$, and $(f)$ is a maximal ideal in $F[x]$.
> - $f(\alpha)=0$, and whenever $g(\alpha)=0$ for some $g\in F[x]$, then $f\mid g$.
> $\quad$

*Proof*  The equivalence of the first two statements, and the last two statements are straightforward. We will prove 

> [!definition] Minimal Polynomial & Degree of an Algebraic Element
> The unique monic polynomial in the above proposition is called the minimal polynomial of $\alpha$ over $F$, and its degree is called the degree of $\alpha$ over $F$, denoted by $\deg_{F}\alpha$.
> 

> [!definition] Generated Field Extension
> Let $K$ be an extension field of $F$. The subfield of $K$ generated by $F$ and an element $\alpha\in K$ is denoted by $F(\alpha)$. That is, $F(\alpha)$ is the smallest subfield of $K$ containing $F$ and $\alpha$. Similarly, $F(\alpha_{1},\alpha_{2},\dots,\alpha_{k})$ is the smallest subfield containing $F$ and $\alpha_{1},\alpha_{2},\dots,\alpha_{k}$.
> 

> [!remark]
> Note that we denote $F[\alpha]$ as the smallest ring containing $F$ and $\alpha$. It is not necessarily a field. Indeed, $\newcommand{\Frac}{\operatorname{frac}} F(\alpha)\cong \Frac F[\alpha]$.
> 

> [!proposition]
> Let $\alpha$ be an element of an extension field $K/F$ which is algebraic over $F$, and let $f$ be the irreducible polynomial for $\alpha$ over $F$. Then
> - The canonical map $F[x]/(f) \to F[\alpha]$ is an isomorphism, and $F[\alpha]$ is a field. Thus $F[\alpha] = F(\alpha)$.
> - More generally, let $\alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_k$ be elements of an extension field $K/F$, which are algebraic over $F$. The ring $F[\alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_k]$ is equal to the field $F(\alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_k)$.
> $\quad$

*Proof*  Let $\varphi\colon F[x]\to K$ be the map, as $f$ is irreducible, the ideal $(f)$ is maximal, and $(f)\subseteq \ker \varphi$ so $(f)=\ker\varphi$. Then by the first isomorphism theorem, $F[x]/(f)\cong F[\alpha]$, is a field. The second statement follows from induction: $$F[\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_k]=F[\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_{k-1}][\alpha_k]=F(\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_{k-1})\left[\alpha_k\right]=F(\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_n).$$$\square$

> [!proposition] 
> Let $\alpha$ be an algebraic element over $F$, and let $f(x)$ be the irreducible polynomial for $\alpha$ over $F$. If $f(x)$ has degree $n$, i.e., if $\alpha$ has degree $n$ over $F$, then $\{1, \alpha, \ldots, \alpha^{n-1}\}$ is a basis for $F(\alpha)$ as a vector space over $F$. ^e3b5a6

*Proof*  Firstly, it is obvious to see that $F(\alpha)$ is a vector space over $F$, particularly, it is spanned by $\{1,\alpha,\ldots,\alpha^{n-1}\}$. Suppose there is a linear dependence relation $$a_{0}+a_{1}\alpha+\cdots+a_{n-1}\alpha^{n-1}=0,$$where not all $a_{i}$ are zero, then it contradicts the minimality of $f$ by division with remainder. $\square$

<u><b>e.g.</b></u>  Suppose $\omega=e^{2\pi i/3}$. Then $\{1,\omega\}$ is a basis for $\Q(\omega)$ over $\Q$.

> [!remark] 
> It may not be easy to tell whether two algebraic elements $\alpha$ and $\beta$ generate isomorphic field extensions, but the above proposition provides a necessary condition: they must have the same degree over $F$.

> [!definition] Isomorphism of Field Extensions
> Let $K$ and $K'$ be extensions of the same field $F$. An isomorphism $\varphi\colon K \to K'$ that restricts to the identity on the subfield $F$ is called an $F$-isomorphism, or an isomorphism of field extensions. If there exists an $F$-isomorphism $\varphi\colon K \to K'$, $K$ and $K'$ are isomorphic extension fields. ^72b6c5

> [!proposition] 
> Let $F$ be a field, and let $\alpha$ and $\beta$ be elements of field extensions $K/F$ and $L/F$. Suppose that $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are algebraic over $F$. There is an $F$-isomorphism of fields $\sigma\colon F(\alpha) \to F(\beta)$ that sends $\alpha \mapsto \beta$ if and only if the irreducible polynomials for $\alpha$ and $\beta$ over $F$ are equal.

*Proof*  Let the shared irreducible polynomial be $f$, then $$F(\alpha)\cong F[x]/(f)\cong F(\beta)$$under the canonical isomorphisms. $\square$

> [!proposition] 
> Let $\varphi\colon K \to K'$ be an $F$-isomorphism, and let $f\in F[x]$. Let $\alpha$ be a root of $f$ in $K$, and let $\alpha' = \varphi(\alpha)$ be its image in $K'$. Then $\alpha'$ is also a root of $f$.

*Proof*  Suppose $f(x)=\sum_{i=0}^{n}a_{i}x^{i}$, then $$f(\alpha)=\sum_{i=0}^{n}a_{i}\alpha^{i}=0\implies \sum_{i=0}^{n}a_{i}\varphi(\alpha)^{i}=0.$$$\square$

## Degrees of Field Extensions

Now we generalize the idea of [[Fields and Field Extensions#^e3b5a6|the proposition]] to any field extension. Note that a field extension $K$ of $F$ can always be regarded as an $F$-vector space. Addition is the addition law in $K$, and scalar multiplication of an element of $K$ by an element of $F$ is obtained by multiplying these two elements in $K$.

> [!definition] Degree of a Field Extension
>  Suppose $K\supset F$ is a field extension. Then the dimension of $K$, when regarded as an $F$-vector space, is called the degree of the field extension. This degree is denoted by $[K : F ]$ or $\deg_{F} K$.
>  A field extension $K/F$ is a finite extension if its degree is finite. Extensions of degree $2$ are quadratic extensions, those of degree $3$ are cubic extensions, and so on.

> [!lemma] 
> The followings are true:
> - A field extension $K/F$ has degree $1$ if and only if $F = K$.  
> - An element $\alpha$ of a field extension $K$ has degree $1$ over $F$ if and only if $\alpha$ is an element of $F$.
> $\quad$

*Proof*  If $[K:F]=1$, then the set $\{1\}$ is a basis of $K$ over $F$, so $K=F$. If $\alpha\in K$ has degree $1$ over $F$, then its minimal polynomial must be $x-\alpha$, so $\alpha\in F$, and vice versa. $\square$

> [!proposition]
> $F\subset K$ are fields. Suppose $\alpha\in K$ is algebraic over $F$. Then $\deg_{F}(F[\alpha])=n$ if and only if the minimal polynomial of $\alpha$ is of degree $n$. 

*Proof*  Let $f(x)$ be the minimal polynomial of $\alpha$ over $F$. Then $F[\alpha]\cong F[x]/(f(x))$, so $\deg_{F}(F[\alpha])=\deg f(x)=n$. $\square$

> [!definition] Characteristic
> Let $\newcommand{\Z}{\mathbb{Z}}\psi\colon \Z\to R$ be the unique ring homomorphism from the integers to a ring $R$. Then the characteristic of $R$ is the smallest nonnegative integer $n$ such that $\ker\psi=n\Z$. 
> i.e. $n$ is the smallest positive integer such that $n\cdot 1=0$ in $R$, or $n=0$ if no such integer exists. ^2bfbd8

> [!proposition]
> Assume that the field $F$ does not have characteristic $2$, that is, $1 + 1 \ne 0$ in $F$. Then any extension $K$ of degree $2$ over $F$ can be obtained by adjoining a square root: $K = F(\delta)$, where $\delta^2 = d$ is an element of $F$. Conversely, if $\delta$ is an element of a field extension of $F$, and if $\delta^2$ is in $F$ but $\delta$ is not in $F$, then $F(\delta)$ is a quadratic extension of $F$.

> [!remark]+
> However, it is not true that all cubic extensions can be obtained by adjoining a cube root.
> 

> [!theorem]
> If $K\supset F$ is a field extension of $F$, and $\deg_{F} K<\infty$, then every element of $K$ is algebraic over $F$.

*Proof*  Assume there is a transcendental element $a\in K$. Then there holds $$F\subset F[a]\subseteq K.$$Note that $F[a]\cong F[x]$ has infinite degree, so $\deg_{F} K=\infty$, which is a contradiction. $\square$

> [!theorem] Multiplicative Property of the Degree
> Suppose $F \subset K \subset A$, where $F$, $K$ are fields and $A$ is a ring. Then $$\deg_{F}A=\deg_{K}A \cdot \deg_{F} K.$$ ^ff28a3

*Proof*  Suppose $\{a_{1},a_{2},\dots,a_{n}\}$ and $\{b_{1},b_{2},\dots,b_{m}\}$ are bases of $A$ over $K$ and $K$ over $F$ respectively. Then $\{a_{i}b_{j}:1\leq i\leq n, 1\leq j\leq m\}$ is a basis of $A$ over $F$. $\square$

One takeaway from the above theorem is that $\deg_{K}A$ has to divide $\deg_{F}A$. So we have the following corollaries:**

> [!corollary]
> Let $K$ be an extension field of $F$ of prime degree $p$. If an element $\alpha$ of $K$ is not in $F$, then $\alpha$ has degree $p$ over $F$ and $K = F(\alpha)$.

> [!corollary] 
> Let $F\subset K\subset L$ be a sequence of field extensions. If an element $\alpha\in L$ is algebraic over $F$, then $\alpha$ is algebraic over $K$ and $\deg _{K}\alpha$ is at most $\deg_{F} \alpha$. ^69ac85

*Proof*  Let $f$ be the minimal polynomial of $\alpha$ over $F$. Since $F\subset K$, $f$ is also a polynomial over $K$. Since $\alpha$ is a root of $f$, the minimal polynomial of $\alpha$ over $K$ divides $f$, so $\deg_{K}\alpha\leq \deg_{F}\alpha$. $\square$

> [!corollary]
> Let $K$ and $K'$ be finite extensions of $F$, and let $L$ denote the field generated by the two fields $K$ and $F'$ together. Let $[L : F] = k$, $[K : F] = m$ and $[k' : F] = n$. Then $m$ and $n$ divide $k$, and $k \leq mn$.
> <img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/yiran-frank-mao/image_repo/master/Obsidian/field_extensions.svg" style="width:30%;"/>

*Proof*  The multiplicative property shows that $m$ and $n$ divides $k$. Now suppose $K'=F(\beta)$. Then [[Fields and Field Extensions#^69ac85|the corollary]] shows that $\deg_{K}L=\deg_{K}\beta \leq \deg_{F}\beta=n$, similarly $\deg_{K'}L\leq m$, so $k=\deg_{K}L\cdot \deg_{F}K\leq mn$. The general case follows by induction. $\square$

So one can apply the above proposition to help to find the minimal polynomial of an algebraic element over a field.

<u><b>e.g.</b></u>  $\Q[\sqrt{2},i]\supset \Q$ 


## Ruler and Compass Constructions

> [!definition] Ruler and Compass Constructions
> A ruler and compass construction is a method of drawing geometric figures using only a straightedge (ruler) and a compass.
> - Two points in the plane are given to start the construction.
> - The straightedge can be used to draw a line between any two points.
> - The compass can be used to draw a circle with a given center and a point on the circle.
> 
> We say the points of intersection of the lines and circles are constructed points. Points, lines, and circles will be called *constructible* if they can be obtained in finitely many steps, using these rules.

> [!theorem]
> Let $P$ be a constructible point in the plane. Then there is a chain of field extensions $\Q\subset F_1\subset F_2\subset \cdots \subset F_{n}=K\subset \R$ such that the coordinates of $P$ are in $K$, and each extension $F_{i+1}/F_{i}$ is a quadratic extension.

> [!corollary]
> Let $a$ be a constructible real number. Then $a$ is algebraic over $\Q$ and the degree of $a$ over $\Q$ is a power of $2$.
