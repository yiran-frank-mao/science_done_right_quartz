## Ideals

> [!definition] Ideal
> A *left ideal* in a ring $R$ is a subset $I$ of $R$ such that 
> - $0\in R$
> - $I$ is closed under addition
> - If $a\in I$ then $ra\in I$ for all $r\in R$.
>
> Equivalently, it is a non-empty subset such that for $a_1 ,\dots,a_k âˆˆI$ we have $r_1 a_1 +\dots+r_k a_k âˆˆI$ for every $r_1,\dots,r_k âˆˆR$. We write $I\triangleleft R$ in such case. ^395472

> [!remark]+ Ideals to Rings are Normal Subgroups to Groups
> Ideals are the ring-theoretic analogue of [[Homomorphisms, Normal Subgroup & Conjugation#^normal|normal subgroups]] in group theory. Just as normal subgroups allow us to form quotient groups, ideals allow us to form quotient rings.

> [!definition] Generated Ideal
> Let $a_1,\dots,a_n âˆˆ R$. The set $\{ r_1a_1+\dots r_n a_n \mid r_i\in \mathbb{R}\}$ is an ideal in $R$ called the ideal generated by $a_1,\dots,a_n$. The notation is $I = (a_1,a_2\dots,a_n)$. ^233314

> [!definition] Principle Ideal
> An ideal $I$ is principal if it is generated by one element. That is, there exists $a âˆˆ R$ such that $I = \{ra \mid r âˆˆ R\}$. It is denoted $(a) = Ra = aR$. ^4a3278

<u><b>e.g.</b></u>  In $\mathbb{Z}$ every ideal is principal and takes the form $n\mathbb{Z} = (n)$. (Proof see [[Factorization#^22b06c|theorem]]).

> [!definition] Product Ideal
> For $I,J\triangleleft R$, define the product ideal $IJ$ as $$IJ=\left\{ \sum_{i=1}^n x_iy_i \mid n\in \mathbb{N}, x_i\in I,y_i\in J  \right\}.$$

> [!proposition]
> If $I,J\triangleleft R$ are ideals so are $I\cap J$ and $IJ$.
