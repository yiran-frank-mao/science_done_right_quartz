> [!definition] Unitary Group
> A *unitary group* consists of all unitary operators on a complex [[Vector Spaces#^f4b63e|vector space]]: $$\newcommand{\U}{\mathrm{U}}\U_{n}:=\{A\in\GL_{n}(\C)\mid A^{*}A=I_{n}\},$$where $^{*}$ denotes the conjugate transpose.
> The *special unitary group* is $\newcommand{\SL}{\mathrm{SL}}\newcommand{\SU}{\mathrm{SU}}\SU_{n}=\SL_{n}(\C)\cap\U_{n}$. ^f61f3c

## Projective Unitary Group

> [!definition] Projective Unitary Group
> Suppose $V$ is a complex [[Vector Spaces#^f4b63e|vector space]], then the projective unitary group $\newcommand{\PU}{\mathrm{PU}}\PU(V)$ is defined as the quotient of $U(V)$ by the scalar phases. That is, 
> $$ \PU(V):= U(V)/ \sim, $$
> where two [[Hilbert Spaces#^68e362|unitary]] operators $T_{1} \sim T_{2}$ if they differ only by a scalar phase factor.
> Its [[Lie Algebra#^5007ba|Lie algebra]] is denoted as $\newcommand{\pu}{\mathfrak{pu}}\pu(V)$.
> 

> [!proposition]
> $\pu(V)$ is the [[Lie Algebra#^5007ba|Lie algebra]] of traceless skew-Hermitian operators. Moreover, $\u(V)\cong \pu(V) \oplus \R$.
