## Poincaré–Birkhoff–Witt Theorem

> [!definition] Universal Enveloping Algebra
> A *universal enveloping algebra* of a [[Lie Algebra#^5007ba|Lie algebra]] $\newcommand{\g}{\mathfrak{g}}\g$ is a pair $(U(\g),i)$ where $U(\g)$ is a Lie algebra that is also a [[Noncommutative Rings#^2c3d07|unital associative algebra]] with the standard Lie bracket (i.e. commutator of multiplication), and $i\colon \g\to U(\g)$ is a Lie algebra homomorphism. 
> It satisfies the following universal property: for any associative algebra $A$ with the standard Lie  bracket and any Lie algebra homomorphism $\phi\colon \g\to A$, there exists a unique associative algebra homomorphism $\tilde{\phi}\colon U(\g)\to A$ such that the following diagram commutes:
> <img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/yiran-frank-mao/image_repo/master/Obsidian/universal_enveloping_algebra.svg" alt="https://q.uiver.app/#q=WzAsMyxbMCwwLCJVKFxcbWF0aGZyYWt7Z30pIl0sWzAsMiwiXFxtYXRoZnJha3tnfSJdLFsyLDIsIkEiXSxbMSwwXSxbMCwyLCIhXFx0aWxkZXtcXHBoaX0iLDAseyJzdHlsZSI6eyJib2R5Ijp7Im5hbWUiOiJkYXNoZWQifX19XSxbMSwyLCJcXHBoaSIsMl1d" style="width:20%;"/>
> In other words, $U(\g)$ is the "most general" associative algebra that $\g$ can map to via Lie algebra homomorphisms. ^3bf4c7

The existence of universal enveloping algebras can be shown using the tensor algebra construction and taking an appropriate quotient: Let $J$ be the two-sided ideal of the [[Tensors and Tensor Products#^b6f3e2|tensor algebra]] $T(\g)$ generated by elements of the form $x\otimes y - y\otimes x - [x,y]$ for all $x,y\in \g$. Then the universal enveloping algebra of $\g$ is given by the quotient algebra $U(\g) = T(\g)/J$, together with the canonical map $i\colon \g \to U(\g)$ that sends each element of $\g$ to its equivalence class in the quotient.
Furthermore, a concrete description of universal enveloping algebras can be given by the Poincaré–Birkhoff–Witt theorem:

> [!theorem] Poincaré–Birkhoff–Witt Theorem
> Suppose $\g$ is a [[Lie Algebra#^5007ba|Lie algebra]] over $F$ with a [[Preorder, Partial Order and Posets#^a8688d|totally ordered]] basis $\{x_{1},x_{2},\cdots\}$. Then its universal enveloping algebra $U(\g)$ exists and has a basis consisting of the ordered monomials. That is, the set of all elements of the form $x_{i_1}\ldots x_{i_n}$ for $i_{1}\leq i_{2}\leq\cdots\leq i_{n}$ and some $n$ is a basis of $U(\g)$. 
> In other words, the [[Grading and Filtration#^ce0272|associated graded algebra]] $\newcommand{\gr}{\operatorname{gr}}\gr U(\g)$ is isomorphic to the symmetric algebra $S(\g)$. ^28e157

*Proof Sketch*  It is clear that the ordered monomials span $U(\g)$, because any monomial that is not well ordered can be rewritten using the relations in the ideal $J$ to express it as a linear combination of well-ordered monomials. The non-trivial part is to show that these ordered monomials are linearly independent.
We utilize the filtration of the tensor algebra $T(\g)$ by degree:$$T(\g) = \bigoplus_{n=0}^{\infty} T^n(\g).$$Let $\iota_{1}\colon F\oplus T^{1}(\g) \hookrightarrow T(\g)$ be the inclusion map. Then $\iota_{1}$ annihilates the ideal $J \cap (F\oplus T^{1}(\g))$, so it induces a well-defined map $\tilde{\iota}_{1}\colon F\oplus T^{1}(\g)/(J \cap (F\oplus T^{1}(\g))) \to U(\g)$. Let us denote $T^{\leq n}(\g) := \bigoplus_{k=0}^{n} T^{k}(\g)$ for each non-negative integer $n$.
For the induction hypothesis, assume we have constructed maps $\iota_{k}\colon T^{\leq k}(\g) \to U(\g)$ annihilating on $J\cap T^{\leq k}(\g)$ for all $k < n$ such that their images are spanned by ordered monomials of degree at most $k$. Then we define $\iota_{n}\colon T^{\leq n}(\g) \hookrightarrow T(\g)$ recursively by $\iota_{n}|_{T^{\leq n-1}(\g)}=\iota_{n-1}$ and $$\iota_{n}(x_{i_{1}}\cdots x_{i_{n}})=\begin{cases} x_{i_{1}}\cdots x_{i_{n}} \quad& \text{if } i_{1}\leq\cdots\leq i_{n}, \\ \iota_{n}(x_{i_{1}}\cdots x_{i_{j+1}}x_{i_{j}}\cdots x_{i_{n}})+\iota_{n-1}(x_{i_{1}}\cdots [x_{i_{j}},x_{i_{j+1}}]\cdots x_{i_{n}}) \quad& \text{otherwise}, \end{cases}$$where $i_{j}$ is the first index with $i_{j}>i_{j+1}$.
The goal is to show that $\iota_{n}$ annihilates $J\cap T^{\leq n}(\g)$. Instead of doing this in full rigor, we illustrate the idea when $n=3$ and $\iota_{2}$ annihilates $J\cap T^{\leq 2}(\g)$. Suppose $x_{1},x_{2},x_{3}\in \g$ are basis vectors. Then we consider the worst case where the monomial is in completely reversed order. We have $$\begin{aligned} & \iota_{3}(x_{3}x_{2}x_{1}) - \iota_{3}(x_{3}x_{1}x_{2}) - \iota_{3}(x_{3}[x_{2},x_{1}]) \\ =& \iota_{3}(x_{2}x_{3}x_{1}) + \iota_{2}([x_{3},x_{2}]x_{1}) - \iota_{3}(x_{1}x_{3}x_{2}) - \iota_{2}([x_{3},x_{1}]x_{2}) - \iota_{2}(x_{3}[x_{2},x_{1}]) \\ =& \iota_{3}(x_{2}x_{1}x_{3}) + \iota_{2}(x_{2}[x_{3},x_{1}]) + \iota_{2}([x_{3},x_{2}]x_{1}) \\ & - \iota_{3}(x_{1}x_{2}x_{3}) - \iota_{2}(x_{1}[x_{3},x_{2}]) - \iota_{2}([x_{3},x_{1}]x_{2}) - \iota_{2}(x_{3}[x_{2},x_{1}]) \\ =& \iota_{2}([x_{2},[x_{3},x_{1}]]) + \iota_{2}([[x_{3},x_{2}],x_{1}]) - \iota_{2}([x_{3},[x_{2},x_{1}]]) \\ =& 0, \end{aligned}$$where the last equality follows from the Jacobi identity. The general case can be handled similarly by repeated application of the Jacobi identity.
Consequently, $\iota_{n}$ induces a well-defined map $\tilde{\iota}_{n}\colon T^{\leq n}(\g)/(J \cap T^{\leq n}(\g)) \to U(\g)$ whose image is spanned by ordered monomials of degree at most $n$. By taking the direct limit as $n\to \infty$, we obtain a map $\iota\colon T(\g)\to U(\g)$ annihilating $J$, so induces $\tilde{\iota}\colon U(\g) \to U(\g)$. Since $\iota$ acts as the identity on ordered monomials, the subalgebra generated by ordered monomials intersects $J$ trivially. Thus, the ordered monomials are linearly independent in $U(\g)$. $\square$

> [!remark]
> As stated in the theorem, equivalently, the PBW theorem can be formulated abstractly as the isomorphism $\gr U(\g) \cong S(\g)$. Let us verify this from the concrete version. Recall that the symmetric algebra $S(\g)$ is defined as the quotient of the tensor algebra $T(\g)$ by the ideal generated by elements of the form $x\otimes y - y\otimes x$ for all $x,y\in \g$. That is, $S(\g)$ is the smallest commutative algebra containing $\g$, so the order of multiplication does not matter in $S(\g)$, and $S(\g)$ is isomorphic to the subalgebra generated by ordered monomials.
> On the other hand, $U(\g)=T(\g)/J$, so $U(\g)$ has a natural filtration induced from the degree filtration of $T(\g)$: $$I_{0} \subseteq I_{1} \subseteq I_{2} \subseteq \cdots,$$where $I_{n}$ is the image of $T^{\leq n}(\g)$ under the quotient map $T(\g)\to U(\g)$. Then the associated graded algebra $\gr U(\g)$ has components given by $\gr_{n} U(\g) = I_{n}/I_{n-1}$. Note that as $J$ has been quotiented out, the relation $x\otimes y - y\otimes x=[x,y]$ holds for all $x,y\in\g$, so in $I_{n}$, we have $$x_{i_{1}}x_{i_{2}}\cdots x_{i_{j}}x_{i_{j+1}}\cdots x_{i_{n}}= x_{i_{1}}x_{i_{2}}\cdots x_{i_{j+1}}x_{i_{j}}\cdots x_{i_{n}}+x_{i_{1}}x_{i_{2}}\cdots [x_{i_{j}},x_{i_{j+1}}]\cdots x_{i_{n}}.$$ 
> However, the last term $x_{i_{1}}x_{i_{2}}\cdots [x_{i_{j}},x_{i_{j+1}}]\cdots x_{i_{n}}$ lies in $I_{n-1}$ because the bracket $[x_{i_{j}},x_{i_{j+1}}]$ can be expressed as a linear combination of basis elements of $\g$, so the entire product has degree at most $n-1$. Therefore, in the quotient $I_{n}/I_{n-1}$, we have $$x_{i_{1}}x_{i_{2}}\cdots x_{i_{j}}x_{i_{j+1}}\cdots x_{i_{n}} = x_{i_{1}}x_{i_{2}}\cdots x_{i_{j+1}}x_{i_{j}}\cdots x_{i_{n}},$$which shows that multiplication in $\gr U(\g)$ is commutative.
> Therefore, by the universal property of the symmetric algebra, there exists a unique graded algebra morphism $\varphi\colon S(\g)\to \gr U(\g)$ extending the identity on $\g$. PBW in ordered-monomial form says the elements $x_{i_{1}}x_{i_{2}}\cdots x_{i_{n}}$ with $i_{1}\leq\cdots\leq i_{n}$ form a basis of $U(\g)$, and any word can be rewritten into an ordered word plus terms of strictly lower filtration degree. Passing to $\gr$, the lower-degree terms vanish, so $\varphi(x_{i_{1}}\cdots x_{i_{n}})$ equals the symbol of the ordered word, showing $\{\varphi(x_{i_{1}}\cdots \varphi (x_{i_{n}})\}_{i_{1}\leq\cdots\leq i_{n}}$ spans $\gr_{n}U(\g)$.
> Moreover, if a nontrivial linear combination of symbols of ordered monomials of length $n$ vanished in $\gr_{n}U(\g)$, the corresponding linear combination in $U(\g)$ would lie in $I_{n-1}$ contradicting that ordered monomials of fixed length are part of a basis of $U(\g)$. Hence those symbols are linearly independent, and $\varphi$ is an isomorphism on each graded piece, so $\varphi$ is an isomorphism of graded algebras.
> 

> [!corollary]
> The canonical map of a Lie algebra to its universal enveloping algebra is an [[Relations and Functions#^042daf|injection]].

> [!proposition]
> Suppose $\g$ and $\newcommand{\h}{\mathfrak{h}}\h$ are Lie algebras over a field $F$. Then we have the following canonical isomorphism:
> $$U(\g\oplus \h) \cong U(\g) \otimes U(\h).$$
> 

## Modules over a Lie Algebra

Now, every Lie algebra has a universal enveloping algebra, which is in particular a ring. So we can talk about modules over it:

> [!definition] Module over a Lie Algebra
> Suppose $\g$ is a [[Lie Algebra#^5007ba|Lie algebra]] over a [[Ring, Field and Integral Domain#^575174|field]] $F$. A *module over $\g$* is a [[Modules#^697aa9|module]] over its universal enveloping algebra $U(\g)$. 
> Almost equivalently, it is an $F$-[[Vector Spaces#^f4b63e|vector space]] $M$ together with a scalar multiplication $\g\times M\to M$, $(x,v)\mapsto x\cdot v$ satisfying:
> - $(ax+by) \cdot v= a(x\cdot v) + b(y\cdot v)$;
> - $x\cdot (av + bw) = a (x\cdot v) + b(x\cdot w)$;
> - $[x,y]\cdot v = x\cdot (y\cdot v) - y\cdot (x\cdot v)$,
> 
> for all $x,y\in \g$, $v,w\in M$ and $a,b\in F$. ^217488

To make the "almost equivalence" precise, given a module $M$ over $U(\g)$, we define the scalar multiplication of $\g$ on $M$ by restricting the action of $U(\g)$ to $\g \subseteq U(\g)$. Conversely, given a vector space $M$ with a scalar multiplication of $\g$ satisfying the above properties, we can uniquely extend this action to all of $U(\g)$ using the universal property, thereby making $M$ into a module over $U(\g)$.

> [!remark]+ Modules over a Lie algebra are Exactly Representations
> A representation of a [[Lie Algebra#^5007ba|Lie algebra]] $L$ is a homomorphism $\newcommand{\gl}{\mathfrak{gl}}\rho\colon L\to \gl (V)$ for some [[Vector Spaces#^f4b63e|vector space]] $V$. This is equivalent to giving $V$ the structure of a module over $L$ by defining $x\cdot v := \rho(x)(v)$ for $x\in L$ and $v\in V$. Therefore, we shall often use the terms "module over a Lie algebra" and "representation of a Lie algebra" interchangeably.

## The Hopf Algebra Structure

> [!proposition]
> The universal enveloping algebra of a Lie algebra carries a natural structure of a cocommutative [[Hopf Algebra#^ad39af|Hopf algebra]]. Specifically, we define
> - comultiplication $\Delta\colon U(\g)\to U(\g)\otimes U(\g)$ by $\Delta(x)=x\otimes 1 + 1 \otimes x$ for all $x\in \g$ and $\Delta(1)=1\otimes 1$,
> - counit $e\colon U(\g)\to F$ by $e(x)=0$ for all $x\in \g$ and $e(1)=1$,
> - antipode $s\colon U(\g)\to U(\g)$ by $s(x)=-x$ for all $x\in \g$ and $s(1)=-1$.
> 
> In particular, $\g$ is precisely the Lie algebra generated by Lie-like elements, i.e., $\g=P(U(\g))$. ^966336

*Proof*  We verify the axioms of a Hopf algebra one by one. First, the counitality laws hold because for any $x\in \g$, $$\begin{aligned} e\otimes \id (\Delta(x))=e\otimes \id (x\otimes 1 + 1 \otimes x)=1\otimes x=\lambda^{-1}(x), \\ \id \otimes e (\Delta(x))=\id \otimes e (x\otimes 1 + 1 \otimes x)=x\otimes 1=\rho^{-1}(x). \end{aligned}$$Next, the coassociativity laws hold because for any $x\in \g$, $$\begin{aligned} (\Delta \otimes \id)(\Delta(x))&=\Delta \otimes \id (x\otimes 1 + 1 \otimes x) \\ &= (x\otimes 1 + 1 \otimes x)\otimes 1 + 1\otimes 1 \otimes x \\ &= \id \otimes \Delta (x\otimes 1 + 1 \otimes x) \\ &= (\id \otimes \Delta)(\Delta(x)). \end{aligned}$$Finally, the antipode axiom holds because for any $x\in \g$, $$\begin{aligned} m\circ (\id \otimes s ) \circ \Delta (x)&=m\circ (\id \otimes s)(x\otimes 1 + 1 \otimes x) \\ &= m(x\otimes (-1) + 1 \otimes (-x)) \\ &=-x + (-x), \\ m\circ (s\otimes \id ) \circ \Delta (x) &=m\circ (s\otimes \id)(x\otimes 1 + 1 \otimes x) \\ &= m((-x)\otimes 1 + (-1) \otimes x) \\ &=-x + (-x) .\end{aligned}$$Furthermore, the cocommutativity holds because for any $x\in \g$, $$\tau \circ \Delta (x) = \tau (x\otimes 1 + 1 \otimes x) = 1 \otimes x + x \otimes 1 = \Delta (x),$$where $\tau\colon U(\g)\otimes U(\g)\to U(\g)\otimes U(\g)$ is the twist map defined by $\tau(a\otimes b)=b\otimes a$. $\square$
