Goodman and Wallach proved the following theorem in 1984, which was originally conjectured by V. Kac:

> [!theorem] Integrability Theorem
> Every unitary highest weight projective representation of $\mathfrak{X}(S^{1})$ integrates to a continuous projective representation of $\newcommand{\Diffgroup}{\mathrm{Diff}^{+}(S^{1})}\Diffgroup$.
> 

*Proof*  Since every UHW projective representation can be completely reduced to irreducible submodules isomorphic to $L(h,c)$, once every $L(h,c)$ integrates, we can integrate an arbitrary module by taking the (Hilbert) direct sum of the implementers on its irreducible summands. Now we show every unitary $L(h,c)$ is integrable step by step using the phase-shift condition.
1. $L(h,1)$ is integrable. 
2. $(\pi, V)$ satisfies the phase-shift condition iff $(\pi^{\otimes n}, V^{\otimes n})$ satisfies for some $n\in\Z_{\geq 0}$.
3. If $r\in\Q$ is positive, $L(h,c)$ satisfies the phase-shift condition iff $L(rh,rc)$ satisfies.
4. Let $\Sigma=\{(h,c)\in\R^{2}\mid c\geq 1 , h\geq(c-1)/24\}$. If $(h,c)\in\Sigma$ then $L(h,c)$ satisfies the phase-shift condition.
5. If $h>0$, then $L(h,c)$ satisfies the phase-shift condition.
6. $L(0,c)$ satisfies the phase-shift condition for all $c>0$.

Let $\Omega$ be a [[Compactness of Topological Space#^da2511|compact]] neighborhood of $(h,1)$ in $\R^{2}$, we can find a neighborhood $M(\Omega)$ of $L_{0}$ in $\newcommand{\vir}{\mathfrak{vir}}\vir$, and therefore a neighborhood $W(\Omega)=\{\phi : \phi^{*}L_{0} \in M(\Omega)\}$ of the identity in $\Diffgroup$, such that for all $\phi\in W$, $\tilde{\pi}^{\phi}(L_{0})$ is self-adjoint, bounded above and has a unique weight $\mu(\pi, \phi)$ with multiplicity one. Pick a highest weight vector $\newcommand{\F}{\mathcal{F}}v_{0}(\pi,\phi)\in \mathcal{H}^{\infty}(\F)$, we define $\newcommand{\H}{\mathfrak{H}}\F_{\pi}^{\phi}:=\pi^{\phi}(U(\H))\cdot v_{0}(\pi,\phi)$ be the cyclic submodule generated by $v_{0}(\pi,\phi)$. We observe the following facts:
- The inner product on $\mathcal{H}$ is contravariant because $(\phi^{*}x)^{*}=\phi^{*}(x^{*})$.
- If $\tilde{\pi}$ is a UHW representation, then $(\pi,\F_{\pi}^{\id})\cong (\pi^{\phi},\F_{\pi}^{\phi})$ is also a UHW representation. Let $\sigma_{\pi}^{\phi}\colon \F_{\pi}^{\phi} \to \F_{\pi}^{\id}$ be the isomorphism such that $\sigma_{\pi}^{\phi}(v_{0}(\pi,\phi))=1_{\F}$.

In summary, for any "good" $\newcommand{\End}{\operatorname{End}}\pi\colon U(\H)\to \End(\F)$, we have a tuple $(\pi, V, \mathcal{H}^{t}(V), v_{0}, \mu_{0})$ where $\mathcal{H}^{t}(V)$ are scales associated with the contravariant inner product and $I-\tilde{\pi}(L_{0})$.

Now recall that $(\tilde{\rho}_{\lambda},\F)\cong L(h,1)$, let us denote $\F^{\phi}_{\lambda}:=\F^{\phi}_{\rho_{\lambda}}$. Then we can first apply a $\phi$-twist to obtain $(\tilde{\rho}_{\lambda}^{\phi}, \F_{\lambda}^{\phi}, v_{0})$, and then apply a $\phi^{-1}$-twist to obtain $(\tilde{\rho}_{\lambda}^{\phi\phi^{-1}}, \F^{\phi\phi^{-1}}, w_{0})$. Note that $$\tilde{\rho}_{\lambda}^{\phi\phi^{-1}}(x)=\tilde{\rho}_{\lambda}(\phi^{*}(\phi^{-1*}x))=\tilde{\rho}_{\lambda}(x),$$so $w_{0}$ is a scalar multiple of $1_{\F}$, thus $1_{\F}\in\mathcal{H}^{\infty}(\F_{\lambda}^{\phi})$. We claim that $\mathcal{H}^{\infty}(\F_{\lambda}^{\phi})$ is invariant under $\rho_{\lambda}(U(\H))$ and $\F_{\lambda}^{\phi}$ is [[Closure, Interior and Boundary#^b560bf|dense]] in $\mathcal{H}^{\infty}(\F_{\lambda}^{\id})$. Indeed, for any $x\in U(\H)$ and $\rho_{\lambda}^{\phi}(y)v_{0}\in \mathcal{H}^{\infty}(\F_{\lambda}^{\phi})$, $$\rho_{\lambda}(x)\rho_{\lambda}^{\phi}(y)v_{0}=\rho_{\lambda}(x(\phi^{*}y))v_{0}=\rho_{\lambda}(\phi^{*}((\phi^{-1*}x)y))v_{0}=\rho_{\lambda}^{\phi}((\phi^{-1*}x)y)v_{0}$$so it is invariant. To see that it is dense, since $\mathcal{H}^{\infty}(\F_{\lambda}^{\phi})$ contains $1_{\F}$, by [[Modules#^9b8178|the proposition]], $\mathcal{H}^{\infty}(\F_{\lambda}^{\phi})$ contains $\rho_{\lambda}^{\phi}(U(\H))\cdot 1_{\F}$, which is dense in $\mathcal{H}^{\infty}(\F_{\lambda}^{\id})$.
Therefore, by the estimates, $\sigma_{\lambda}^{\phi}$ is continuous at all scales, and extends to a unitary operator $\hat{\sigma}_{\lambda}^{\phi}\colon \mathcal{H}^{t}(\F_{\lambda}^{\phi}) \to \mathcal{H}^{t}(\F_{\lambda}^{\id})$ for all $t\in\R$.
**Step 3: Extend to $D_{0}$**
Let $D_{0}=\{\phi\in \Diffgroup \mid \phi(1)=1\}$. We now consider a local representation $\beta_{\lambda}\colon D_{0} \cap W(\Omega) \to U(\mathcal{H}^{t})$ defined by $\phi\mapsto \hat{\sigma}_{\lambda}^{\phi}$. Since $D_{0}$ is contractible, $\beta_{\lambda}$ induces a unique $\hat{\beta}_{\lambda}\colon D_{0} \to PU(\mathcal{H}^{t})$. It is already a projective representation of $D_{0}$, now we recover the 2-cocycle from the complex line bundle defined by $$L_{\phi}D_{0} = \hat{\beta}_{\lambda}^{\phi^{-1}}([1_{\F}]),$$where $[1_{\F}]$ is the line spanned by $1_{\F}$. As $D_{0}$ is contractible and precompact, this line bundle is locally trivial (thus well-defined). Therefore we can take a global continuous section of $LD_{0}$ with unit norm, say $\phi\mapsto s(\phi)$. Notice that $D_{0}$ is [[Connectedness and Paths#^946cc4|connected]], so it is generated by any neighborhood of identity, in particular, for any $\phi\in D_{0}$, we can choose $\phi_{1},\dots,\phi_{n}\in D_{0}\cap W(\Omega)$ such that $\phi=\phi_{1}\cdots \phi_{n}$. Then we define $$\alpha_{\lambda}(\phi):=\langle \beta_{\lambda}(\phi_{n}^{-1})\beta_{\lambda}(\phi_{n-1}^{-1})\cdots \beta_{\lambda}(\phi_{1}^{-1}) 1_{\F}, s(\phi) \rangle \cdot \beta_{\lambda}^{\phi_{1}}\beta_{\lambda}^{\phi_{2}}\cdots \beta_{\lambda}^{\phi_{n}}. $$Intertwining property and cyclicity of $1_{\F}$ ensure that $\alpha_{\lambda}(\phi)$ is independent of the choice of factorization, and it is a projective representation of $D_{0}$.
**Step 4: Extend to $\Diffgroup$**
Finally, we extend $\alpha_{\lambda}$ to $\Diffgroup$. Note that $\Diffgroup \cong D_{0} \times S^{1}$ through $(\phi, e^{i\theta})\mapsto (z\mapsto e^{i\theta}\phi(z))$. We define $$\hat{\alpha}_{\lambda}(\phi, e^{i\theta}):= \exp\left(i\theta\left(\Phi_{\lambda}(L_{0})+ \frac{\lambda^{2}}{2} I\right)\right) \alpha_{\lambda}(\phi).$$It is straightforward to verify that $\hat{\rho}_{\lambda}$ is a projective representation of $\Diffgroup$ extending $\rho_{\lambda}$. $\square$
