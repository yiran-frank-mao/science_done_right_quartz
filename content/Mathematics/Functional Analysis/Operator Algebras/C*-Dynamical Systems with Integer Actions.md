## Crossed Product C*-Algebras

> [!definition] C*-Dynamical System
> A C*-dynamical system is a triple $(A, G, \alpha)$, where $A$ is a C*-algebra, $G$ is a locally compact group, and $\alpha: G \to \operatorname{Aut}(A)$ is a strongly continuous group homomorphism. In these notes, we focus on the case where $G=\mathbb{Z}$. ^c8a1b2

> [!definition] Crossed Product C*-Algebra
> Given a C*-dynamical system $(A, \mathbb{Z}, \alpha)$, the *crossed product C*-algebra* $A \times_\alpha \mathbb{Z}$ is the universal C*-algebra generated by a copy of $A$ and a unitary $u$ satisfying the covariance relation: $$uau^* = \alpha_1(a) \quad \text{for all } a \in A$$
> The set of finite sums $\sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} a_n u^n$ with $a_n \in A$ forms a dense *-subalgebra.

## The Canonical Expectation

> [!theorem] The Gauge Action
> For each $\lambda \in \mathbb{T} = \{z \in \mathbb{C} : |z|=1\}$, there exists a unique *-automorphism $\rho_\lambda$ of $A \times_\alpha \mathbb{Z}$ determined by:
> - $\rho_\lambda(a) = a$ for all $a \in A$
> - $\rho_\lambda(u) = \lambda u$
> This family of automorphisms $\{\rho_\lambda\}_{\lambda \in \mathbb{T}}$ is called the *gauge action*.

> [!theorem] The Canonical Expectation
> Let $(A, \mathbb{Z}, \alpha)$ be a C*-dynamical system. The map $\phi: A \times_\alpha \mathbb{Z} \to A$ defined by the integral over the gauge action
> $$\phi(x) = \int_{0}^{1} \rho_{e^{2\pi i t}}(x) \dd t$$
> is a faithful conditional expectation from $A \times_\alpha \mathbb{Z}$ onto $A$. ^f0a1c3

*Proof*
We verify the properties of a faithful conditional expectation. Let $x = \sum_n a_n u^n$ be an element in the dense subalgebra of "polynomials".
* **Projection onto A**: We compute $\phi$ on the generators. For $a \in A$, $\phi(a) = \int_0^1 \rho_{e^{2\pi i t}}(a) \dd t = \int_0^1 a \dd t = a$. For the unitary $u^k$,
    $$\phi(u^k) = \int_0^1 \rho_{e^{2\pi i t}}(u^k) \dd t = \int_0^1 (e^{2\pi i t}u)^k \dd t = u^k \int_0^1 e^{2\pi i k t} \dd t = \begin{cases} u^0 = I & k=0 \\ 0 & k \neq 0 \end{cases}$$
    By linearity, for $x = \sum_n a_n u^n$, we have $\phi(x) = \sum_n a_n \phi(u^n) = a_0$. Since the image is in $A$ for the dense subalgebra, by continuity it holds for all of $A \times_\alpha \mathbb{Z}$.
* **Expectation Property**: For $a, b \in A$ and $x \in A \times_\alpha \mathbb{Z}$,
    $$\phi(axb) = \int_0^1 \rho_{e^{2\pi i t}}(axb) \dd t = \int_0^1 a \rho_{e^{2\pi i t}}(x) b \dd t = a \left(\int_0^1 \rho_{e^{2\pi i t}}(x) \dd t\right) b = a\phi(x)b$$
* **Positivity**: If $x \in A \times_\alpha \mathbb{Z}$ is positive, then $\rho_\lambda(x)$ is positive for all $\lambda \in \mathbb{T}$. The integral of a continuous path of positive elements is positive, so $\phi(x) \ge 0$.
* **Faithfulness**: Suppose $\phi(x^*x) = 0$ for $x = \sum_n a_n u^n$. The $u^0$ coefficient of $x^*x = (\sum_m a_m u^m)^*(\sum_n a_n u^n)$ is $\sum_n \alpha_{-n}(a_n^* a_n)$.
    Thus, $\phi(x^*x) = \sum_n \alpha_{-n}(a_n^* a_n) = 0$.
    Since $\alpha_{-n}$ is a *-automorphism, each term $\alpha_{-n}(a_n^* a_n)$ is positive. A sum of positive elements is zero if and only if each element is zero. Thus, $\alpha_{-n}(a_n^* a_n)=0$, which implies $a_n^*a_n=0$, and hence $a_n=0$ for all $n$. So $x=0$. The property extends to the whole algebra by density.
$\square$

## The Rotation Algebra

> [!definition]
> Let $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$. Consider the C*-dynamical system $(C(\mathbb{T}), \mathbb{Z}, \alpha)$ where the action $\alpha$ is rotation:
> $$(\alpha_n f)(z) = f(e^{2\pi i n \theta} z)$$
> The corresponding crossed product C*-algebra $A_\theta := C(\mathbb{T}) \times_\alpha \mathbb{Z}$ is called the **rotation algebra**.

> [!remark] Generators and Relations
> The algebra $C(\mathbb{T})$ is generated by the unitary position operator $v$, where $v(z)=z$. The action on this generator is
> $$(\alpha_1 v)(z) = v(e^{2\pi i \theta} z) = e^{2\pi i \theta} z = e^{2\pi i \theta} v(z)$$
> Let $u$ be the unitary implementing the action in the crossed product. The covariance relation $uvu^* = \alpha_1(v)$ becomes $uvu^* = e^{2\pi i \theta} v$. This is equivalent to the commutation relation:
> $$uv = e^{2\pi i \theta} vu$$
> Thus, $A_\theta$ can be described as the universal C*-algebra generated by two unitaries $u, v$ satisfying this relation.

> [!theorem] Simplicity of the Rotation Algebra
> Let $A_\theta$ be the rotation algebra for $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$.
> 1.  If $\theta$ is **rational**, then $A_\theta$ is not simple.
> 2.  If $\theta$ is **irrational**, then $A_\theta$ is simple (it contains no non-trivial closed two-sided ideals).

*Proof (Irrational case)*
Let $\theta$ be irrational and let $J$ be a non-zero closed two-sided ideal in $A_\theta$.

1.  **Find a non-zero element in $J \cap C(\mathbb{T})$**. Let $x \in J$ be a non-zero element. Then $x^*x$ is a non-zero positive element in $J$. Let $\phi$ be the canonical expectation from [[#^f0a1c3|the theorem above]]. Since $\phi$ is faithful, $a := \phi(x^*x)$ is a non-zero positive element in $C(\mathbb{T})$. It is a standard result of crossed product theory that if $J$ is a non-zero ideal, then $J \cap C(\mathbb{T})$ is also a non-zero ideal of $C(\mathbb{T})$. Let $I = J \cap C(\mathbb{T})$.

2.  **Show $I$ is $\alpha$-invariant**. Let $f \in I$. Then $f \in J$ and $f \in C(\mathbb{T})$. For any $n \in \mathbb{Z}$, we have $\alpha_n(f) = u^n f (u^n)^*$. Since $f \in J$ and $J$ is a two-sided ideal, it follows that $u^n f (u^n)^* \in J$. As $\alpha_n$ is an automorphism of $C(\mathbb{T})$, $\alpha_n(f)$ is also in $C(\mathbb{T})$. Therefore, $\alpha_n(f) \in J \cap C(\mathbb{T}) = I$.

3.  **Use minimality of the action**. $I$ is a non-zero, closed, $\alpha$-invariant ideal of $C(\mathbb{T})$. By Gelfand theory, any closed ideal of $C(\mathbb{T})$ is of the form $\{f \in C(\mathbb{T}) : f|_F = 0\}$ for some closed subset $F \subseteq \mathbb{T}$. Since $I$ is $\alpha$-invariant, the set $F$ must be invariant under the rotation $z \mapsto e^{2\pi i \theta} z$. Because $\theta$ is irrational, the orbit of any point in $\mathbb{T}$ under this rotation is dense in $\mathbb{T}$. If $F$ were non-empty, it would have to contain a dense subset, and being closed, it would have to be all of $\mathbb{T}$. But if $F=\mathbb{T}$, then $I=\{0\}$, which contradicts that $I$ is non-zero. Thus, the only possibility for an invariant closed subset $F$ is $\mathbb{T}$ or $\emptyset$. Since $I \neq \{0\}$, we must have $F = \emptyset$. This implies that $I$ is the entire algebra $C(\mathbb{T})$.

4.  **Conclude $J=A_\theta$**. We have shown that if $J$ is a non-zero ideal, it must contain all of $C(\mathbb{T})$. Since $J$ is an ideal and $C(\mathbb{T}) \subset J$, it must also contain all elements of the form $f u^n$ for $f \in C(\mathbb{T})$ and $n \in \mathbb{Z}$. The linear span of these elements is the dense subalgebra of polynomials. Since $J$ is closed and contains a dense subalgebra of $A_\theta$, we must have $J = A_\theta$.

Thus, $A_\theta$ has no non-trivial closed two-sided ideals and is simple. $\square$
