It is well known that a Lie algebra representation does not, in general, integrate to a representation of the corresponding Lie group. In fact, even under seemingly "perfect" conditions this may fail. Nelson’s counterexample demonstrates that, even when every element of a Lie algebra is essentially skew-adjoint on a common invariant domain in a Hilbert space, the representation need not integrate to a unitary group representation.

## A Sufficient Condition for Essential Skew-Adjointness

Before turning to Nelson’s construction, we first state a lemma that provides a sufficient condition for an operator to be essentially skew-adjoint.

> [!lemma]
> Let $M$ be a [[Manifolds and Atlases#^6ef2ef|smooth manifold]] with a [[Differential Forms#^923fae|volume form]] $\omega$. Let $L^2(M)$ be the space of square-integrable functions with respect to the measure $\mu$ induced by $\omega$. Suppose a vector field $X \in \mathfrak{X}(M)$ is skew-symmetric on the domain of compactly supported smooth functions $C_c^\infty(M)$. Let $\Phi_t$ be the [flow generated by $X$](Integral%20Curves%20and%20Flows#^58f6d4), and define the [[Closure, Interior and Boundary#^cf4e59|closure]] of the set of points where the flow is not defined at time $t$ as $E_t(X) := \overline{\{x \in M \mid \Phi_t(x) \text{ is not defined}\}}$. If for some $t > 0$, $\mu(E_{t})=\mu(E_{-t}) = 0$, then $X$ is essentially skew-adjoint. ^1e63ec

*Proof*  First, we show that the skew-symmetry of $X$ implies that its flow preserves the volume. The skew-symmetry condition is that for all $f, g \in C_c^\infty(M)$, we have $\langle Xf, g \rangle_{L^{2}(M)} = -\langle f, Xg \rangle_{L^{2}(M)}$. This means: $$\newcommand{\d}{\mathrm{d}}\newcommand{\dd}{\,\mathrm{d}} \int_M (Xf)g \dd\mu + \int_M f(Xg) \dd\mu = 0 \implies \int_M X(fg) \dd\mu = 0. $$In terms of the Lie derivative $\mathcal{L}_X$ and the volume form $\omega$, this is $\int_M \mathcal{L}_X(fg\omega) = 0$. Using the product rule, $\mathcal{L}_X(fg\omega) = (\mathcal{L}_X(fg))\omega + fg(\mathcal{L}_X\omega)$. The integral of the first term vanishes, because, by [[Differential Forms#^04ae6c|Cartan's formula]] and [[Integration of Differential Forms#^33d2f3|Stoke's theorem]], we have: $$\begin{aligned}\int_{M} (\mathcal{L}_X(fg))\omega &= \int_{M} \d(i_{X}(fg)\omega)+\int_{M}i_{X}\d((fg)\omega) \\ &= \int_{\partial M} i_{X}(fg)\omega + \int_{M}i_{X}\d((fg)\omega). \end{aligned}$$The first term is zero because $fg\in C_{c}(M)$ vanishes on the boundary $\partial M$. The second term is zero because $\d((fg)\omega)$ is a form of degree $n+1$. This leaves us with: $$ \int_M (fg) \mathcal{L}_X\omega = 0. $$Since this holds for all $f, g \in C_c^\infty(M)$, it must be that $\mathcal{L}_X\omega = 0$. This is the condition for the flow $\Phi_t$ to be volume-preserving.
Now, for $t \in \mathbb{R}$, we define a family of operators $U(t)\colon C_{c}^{\infty}(M)\to C_{c}^{\infty}(M)$ associated with the flow: $$ (U(t)f)(x) := \begin{cases}(\Phi_t^*f)(x) \quad& x \notin E_{t},\\0,\quad& x\in E_{t}.\end{cases}$$Since the flow is volume-preserving and $\mu(E_t)=0$, this operator is bounded on $L^2(M)$: $$ \|U(t)f\|^2 = \int_M |f(\Phi_t(x))|^2 \dd\mu(x) = \int_{\Phi_t^{-1}(M)} |f(y)|^2 \dd\mu(y) \leq \|f\|^2 $$where we performed a [[Integration of Differential Forms#^74d864|change of variables]] $y=\Phi_t(x)$.
For any $f \in C_{c}^{\infty}\left(M \setminus E_{t}\right)^{\perp}$, $f$ must vanish a.e. on $M \setminus E_{t}$, as $\int f g \dd \mu=0$ for all $g \in C_{c}^{\infty}(M\setminus E_{t})$. If $\mu\left(E_{t}\right)=0$, then $C_{c}^{\infty}\left(M \setminus E_{t}\right)^{\perp}=\{0\}$, so $C_{c}^{\infty}\left(M \setminus E_{t}\right)$ is dense in $L^{2}(M)$. Therefore, $X$ is densely defined.
To show $X$ is essentially skew-adjoint, it suffices to show that its [[Unbounded Operators#^db609c|adjoint]] $X^*$ has no real eigenvalues, i.e., $\text{ker}(X^* \pm I) = \{0\}$. Suppose $X^*f = -f$ for some $f \in D(X^*)$, and $\mu(E_{t})=0$ for some $t>0$. For any $g \in C_c^\infty(M \setminus E_t)$, consider the time evolution of the inner product $\langle U(s)g, f \rangle$: $$ \frac{\d}{\d s} \langle U(s)g, f \rangle = \left\langle \frac{\d}{\d s}U(s)g, f \right\rangle = \langle XU(s)g, f \rangle= -\langle U(s)g, X^*f \rangle=\langle U(s)g, f \rangle. $$So $\langle U(s)g, f \rangle = \langle g, f \rangle e^s$. By the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality and the fact that $U(s)$ is bounded, we have:$$ |\langle U(s)g, f \rangle| \leq \|U(s)g\| \|f\| = \|g\| \|f\|.$$It follows that $\|f\|=0$, which implies $f=0$. A similar argument for $X^*f=f$ leads to $\langle U(s)g, f \rangle = \langle g, f \rangle e^{-s}$. By considering the flow backwards in time, using $U(-t)$, which leads to $\|f\|^2 e^t \leq \|f\|^2$, again implying $f=0$. Thus, $\text{ker}(X^* \pm I) = \{0\}$, and $X$ is essentially skew-adjoint. $\square$

## Nelson's Counterexample

Now, we construct the counterexample. Consider a genus-4 [[Manifolds and Atlases#^6ef2ef|smooth manifold]] $M$, defined by the following diagram:
<img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/yiran-frank-mao/image_repo/master/Obsidian/Nelson's_counterexample_construction.svg" alt="https://gist.github.com/yiran-frank-mao/ed3fe79488445de8fa5dc92babf4c3ab" style="width:27%;"/>
That is, a band bounded by two concentric squares, where each point is identified with its opposite, the four vertices are also identified, and the 12 marked points in the above diagram are removed. Finally, we obtain a two-dimensional surface resembling a bunch of bananas, except that each “banana” is a torus:

<img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/yiran-frank-mao/image_repo/master/Obsidian/Nelson's_counterexample_banana.svg" alt="Nelson's_counterexample_banana" style="width:23%;"/>

Let's define an abelian Lie algebra of vector fields on $M$: $$ \mathfrak{g} = \left\{ a\frac{\partial}{\partial x} + b\frac{\partial}{\partial y} \middle| a, b \in \mathbb{R} \right\}. $$These are skew-symmetric operators on the common invariant domain $C_{0}^{\infty}(M)$. For any $X \in \mathfrak{g}$, the flow is not defined on only 7 discrete line segments, i.e., once fixed the slope, there are only 7 possible ways to hit the hole:

<img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/yiran-frank-mao/image_repo/master/Obsidian/Nelson's_counterexample_flow.svg" alt="Nelson's_counterexample_flow" style="width:24%;"/>

Therefore, $\mu(E_{t}(X))=0$ for all $X\in\mathfrak{g}$. By [[Nelson's Counterexample#^1e63ec|the lemma]], every operator $X \in \mathfrak{g}$ is essentially skew-adjoint.
However, this collection of operators does not generate a unitary group representation. Let $\hat{X} = \overline{\frac{\partial}{\partial x}}$ and $\hat{Y} = \overline{\frac{\partial}{\partial y}}$, being the [[Unbounded Operators^|closure]] of unit vector fields (as operators).
Consider a function $f$ supported on the lower-left square $[0,1]\times[0,1]$. Applying $e^{\hat{Y}}$ shifts its support in the $y$-direction to $[0,1]\times[1,2]$, which lies on one of the tori. Subsequently, applying $e^{\hat{X}}$ preserves this torus, so $e^{\hat{X}}e^{\hat{Y}}f$ has support $[0,1]\times[1,2]$. On the other hand, applying $e^{\hat{X}}$ first shifts the support to $[1,2]\times[0,1]$, and then $e^{\hat{Y}}$ preserves that region, so $e^{\hat{Y}}e^{\hat{X}}f$ has support $[1,2]\times[0,1]$. Thus $e^{\hat{X}}e^{\hat{Y}} \neq e^{\hat{Y}}e^{\hat{X}}$.

This demonstrates that even when each element of a Lie algebra is individually well-behaved (essentially skew-adjoint), additional conditions are needed to ensure the existence of an associated group representation. These additional conditions are then the [[Nelson's Commutator Theorem#^edbb20|Nelson's commutator theorem]].
