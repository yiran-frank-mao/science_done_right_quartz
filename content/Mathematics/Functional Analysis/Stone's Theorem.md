## Exponential of Operators

> [!definition] Exponential of a Self-adjoint Operator
> Suppose $T$ is a [[Symmetric and Self-Adjoint Operators#^450e4c|self-adjoint]] operator on a [[Hilbert Spaces#^ae0212|Hilbert space]] $\newcommand{\H}{\mathcal{H}}\H$. If $T$ is bounded, we can simply define the exponential of $T$ as $$e^{T}=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{T^{n}}{n!}.$$If $T$ is unbounded, we can define the exponential of $T$ using the spectral theorem,

*Proof*  We shall check it is well-defined when $T$ is bounded. Suppose $T\in B(\H)$, define $\{S_{k}\}_{k=0}^{\infty}\subset B(\H)$ as $S_{k}=\sum_{j=0}^{k} \frac{T^{j}}{j!}$. Then for any $n,m\geq N$, we have $$\|S_{n}-S_{m}\|_{\text{op}}\leq \sum_{j=n+1}^{m}\frac{\|T\|^{j}}{j!} \to 0,\quad \text{as } N\to 0,$$because the exponential series ([[Complete Metric Space#^179eeb|Cauchy]]) converges in $\newcommand{\R}{\mathbb{R}}\R$ (ref. [[Complete Metric Space#^ad57d2|theorem]]). Therefore, $\{S_{k}\}_{k=0}^{\infty}$ is also [[Complete Metric Space#^179eeb|Cauchy]], thus converges in the Banach space $B(\H)$. $\square$

> [!proposition]
> Let $T$ be a self-adjoint operator on a [[Hilbert Spaces#^ae0212|Hilbert space]] $\H$. Define $U(t)=e^{it T}$, then
> - For each $t\in\R$, $U(t)$ is [[Hilbert Spaces#^68e362|unitary]] and $U(t+s)=U(t)U(s)$;
> - If $\varphi\in \H$, then $U(t)\varphi \to U(t_{0})\varphi$ as $t\to t_{0}$, i.e. $U(t)$ is strongly continuous;
> - For $\psi\in D(T)$, $\frac{U(t)\psi-\psi}{t}\to iT\psi$ as $t\to 0$;
> - If $\lim_{t\to 0} \frac{U(t)\psi-\psi}{t}$ exists, then $\psi\in D(T)$.
> $\quad$

*Proof*  
1. Consider the functional calculus $\Phi\colon B_{b}(\R)\to B(\H)$ of $T$. Let $f_{r}\in B_{b}(\R)$ be the map $x\mapsto e^{ir x}$. Then since $\Phi_{T}$ is a [$\ast$-homomorphism](C*-Algebras#^1a6445), we have $$U(t+s)=\Phi(f_{t+s})=\Phi(f_{t}\cdot f_{s})=\Phi(f_{t})\cdot \Phi(f_{s})=U(s)U(t).$$Moreover, $$U(t)^{*}U(t)=\Phi(f_{t}^{*})U(t)=\Phi(f_{-t})U(t)=U(-t)U(t)=U(0)=I.$$

## The Stone's Theorem

Conversely, given a [[Groups, Order and Subgroups#^6e0960|group]] of unitary operators, can it be represented as the exponential? Stone's theorem answers this question:

> [!theorem] Stone's Theorem
> Let $U(t)$ be a strongly continuous one-parameter [[Hilbert Spaces#^68e362|unitary]] [[Groups, Order and Subgroups#^6e0960|group]] on a [[Hilbert Spaces#^ae0212|Hilbert space]] $\H$. Then, there is a self-adjoint operator $\hat{H}$ on $\H$ so that $$U(t)=e^{it\hat{H}}$$ for all $t\in\R$. In this case, we call $\hat{H}$ the *infinitesimal generator* of the unitary group $U(t)$.
 
*Proof*  For any $\varphi\in\H$, and $f\in C^{\infty}_{0}(\R)$, we define $$\newcommand{\dd}{\,\mathrm{d}}\varphi_{f}:=\int_{\R} f(t) U(t)\varphi \dd t,$$as a vector-valued integral, taking values in $\H$, because $U(t)$ is strongly continuous. Define the following subset of $\H$: $$\newcommand{\span}{\mathrm{span}}D:=\span \{ \varphi_{f} \in \H \mid \varphi \in\H, f\in C^{\infty}_{0}(\R)  \}.$$Now we claim that $D$ is [[Closure, Interior and Boundary#^b560bf|dense]] in $\H$. In fact, let $j_{\varepsilon}$ be the approximate identity, then $\varphi=\int_{\R} j_{\varepsilon}\varphi$, and we have $$\|\varphi_{j_{\varepsilon}}-\varphi\|=\left\| \int_{\R} j_{\varepsilon}(t) U(t)\varphi \dd t-\varphi \right\|=\left\| \int_{\R} j_{\varepsilon}(t) (U(t)\varphi-\varphi) \dd t \right\|\leq \left\| \int_{\R} j_{\varepsilon} \right\|\cdot \sup_{t\in[-\varepsilon,\varepsilon]}\| U(t)\varphi -\varphi \|.$$Notice that by strong continuity of $U(t)$, we have $$\lim_{\varepsilon\to 0} \sup_{t\in[-\varepsilon,\varepsilon]}\| U(t)\varphi -\varphi \|=\lim_{t\to 0}\| U(t)\varphi -\varphi \|=0,$$so $\varphi_{j_{\varepsilon}}\to \varphi$ as $\varepsilon\to 0$. Hence, any $\varphi\in\H$ has $\varphi_{j_{\varepsilon}}\in D$ that can be arbitrarily close to $\varphi$, that is $D$ is dense in $\H$.
Now we define $A\colon D\to D$ by $A\varphi_{f}:=\lim_{s\to 0}\left(\frac{U(s)-I}{is}\right)\varphi_{f}$, and claim that its closure is our desired operator. We first show it is symmetric. Observe that $$\begin{aligned}\lim_{s\to 0}\left(\frac{U(s)-I}{s}\varphi_{f}\right)&=\lim_{s\to 0} \int_{\R} f(t)\left(\frac{U(s+t)-U(t)}{s}\right) \varphi\dd t \\&= \lim_{s\to 0} \int_{\R}\frac{f(t)}{s}(U(s+t)-U(t))\varphi \dd t \\ &= \lim_{s\to 0} \left( \int_{\R} \frac{f(t)}{s}U(s+t)\varphi\dd t- \int_{\R}\frac{f(t)}{s}U(t)\varphi\dd t \right) \\&= \lim_{s\to 0} \left( \int_{\R} \frac{f(\tau-s)}{s}U(\tau)\varphi\dd \tau- \int_{\R} \frac{f(\tau)}{s}U(\tau)\varphi \dd\tau\right) \\&= \lim_{s\to 0} \int_{\R} \frac{f(\tau-s)-f(\tau)}{s} U(\tau)\varphi \dd \tau \\ &=\varphi_{-f'},\end{aligned}$$where the last equation holds because the derivative of compactly supported smooth $f$ is bounded, so the [[Integration on Measure Spaces#^f80aa2|bounded convergence theorem]] applies. Thus $A\varphi_{f}=\lim_{s\to 0}\left(\frac{U(s)-I}{is}\varphi_{f}\right)=i^{-1}\cdot \varphi_{-f'}$. Similarly, we can also compute and show that $A\varphi_{f}=\lim_{s\to 0}\left(\frac{I-U(-s)}{is}\varphi_{f}\right)$. For any $\varphi_{f}, \psi_{g}\in D$, we have $$\langle A\varphi_{f},\psi_{g}\rangle =\lim_{s\to 0} \left\langle \frac{U(s)-I}{is}\varphi_{f}, \psi_{g} \right\rangle=\lim_{s\to 0}\left\langle\varphi_{f}, \frac{I-U(-s)}{s}\psi_{g} \right\rangle = \langle \varphi_{f},A\psi_{g}\rangle. $$Therefore, $A$ is symmetric. 
Moreover, $A$ satisfies $\newcommand{\ddf}[2]{\frac{\mathrm{d}#1}{\mathrm{d}#2}}iA(U(t_{0})\varphi_{f})=\left.\ddf{}{t}\right|_{t=t_{0}}(U(t)\varphi_{f})$. We can see this by first identifying $U(t_{0})\varphi_{f}=\varphi_{\tilde{f}_{t_{0}}}$ (so $U(t)(D)\subseteq D$) where $\tilde{f}_{t_{0}}(t):=f(t-t_{0})$. Then similarly compute: $$\begin{aligned}\left.\ddf{}{t}\right|_{t=t_{0}}(U(t)\varphi_{f})&=\lim_{s\to 0} \int_{\R} \frac{f(\tau-t-s)-f(\tau-t)}{s} U(\tau)\varphi_{f} \dd\tau\\ &=\varphi_{-\tilde{f}_{t_{0}}}=iA\varphi_{\tilde{f}_{t_{0}}}=iA(U(t_{0})\varphi).\end{aligned}$$
Now we show that it is essentially self-adjoint by showing that $\ker(A^* \pm iI) = \{0\}$, so has a unique self-adjoint extension. Let $u \in \mathcal{H}$ such that $A^*u = iu$. For any $\varphi \in D$, consider the complex function $h(t) = \langle U(t)\varphi, u \rangle$. Its derivative is: $$ \ddf{}{t} h(t) = \langle iAU(t)\varphi, u \rangle = \langle U(t)\varphi, -iA^*u \rangle = \langle U(t)\varphi, -i(iu) \rangle = \langle U(t)\varphi, u \rangle = h(t).$$The solution to this differential equation is $h(t) = h(0)e^t = \langle \varphi, u \rangle e^t$. Note that since $U(t)$ is unitary, $h(t)$ is bounded (by the [[Inner Products#^aec3f8|Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality]]): $$ |h(t)| = |\langle U(t)\varphi, u \rangle| \le \|U(t)\varphi\| \|u\| = \|\varphi\| \|u\|.$$So, we must have $|\langle \varphi, u \rangle e^t| \le \|\varphi\| \|u\|$ for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$. This can only be true if $\langle \varphi, u \rangle = 0$. Since this holds for all $\varphi \in D$ and $D$ is [[Closure, Interior and Boundary#^b560bf|dense]] in the Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$, we must have $u=0$ (ref. [[Orthogonality and Bounded Linear Maps#^6ac7cd|corollary]]). A similar argument shows that if $A^*u = -iu$, then $u=0$. Thus, $\ker(A^* \pm iI) = \{0\}$. Therefore, the symmetric operator $A$ is essentially self-adjoint. It admits a unique self-adjoint extension $\hat{H}:=\bar{A} = A^{**}$.
It remains to show that $U(t) = e^{it\bar{A}}$. For any $\varphi \in D\subset D(\bar{A})$, let us define a function $w(t)$: $$ w(t) := U(t)\varphi - e^{it\bar{A}}\varphi $$Note that $w(0) = U(0)\varphi - e^{i0\bar{A}}\varphi = \varphi - \varphi = 0$. Now, we compute the derivative of $w(t)$. Since $\varphi \in D$, it follows that $U(t)\varphi \in D$ and $e^{it\bar{A}}\varphi \in D(\bar{A})$. On the domain $D(A)=D$, the operator $A$ and its extension $\bar{A}$ coincide. Hence, $$ w'(t) = \ddf{}{t}(U(t)\varphi - e^{it\bar{A}}\varphi) = iAU(t)\varphi - i\bar{A}e^{it\bar{A}}\varphi=i\bar{A}U(t)\varphi - i\bar{A}e^{it\bar{A}}\varphi =i\bar{A}w(t).$$Now, let's look at the derivative of the squared norm of $w(t)$: $$ \begin{aligned}
\ddf{}{t}\|w(t)\|^2 &= \ddf{}{t}\langle w(t), w(t) \rangle\\ &= \langle w'(t), w(t) \rangle + \langle w(t), w'(t) \rangle\\&=\langle i\bar{A}w(t), w(t) \rangle + \langle w(t), i\bar{A}w(t) \rangle\\&= i\langle \bar{A}w(t), w(t) \rangle - i\langle \bar{A}w(t), w(t) \rangle\\&=0.\end{aligned} $$This implies that $\|w(t)\|$ is a constant, $\|w(t)\| = \|w(0)\| = 0$ for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$. Thus, $w(t) = 0$ for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$. This means: $U(t)\varphi = e^{it\bar{A}}\varphi,$ for all $t \in \mathbb{R},$ and $\varphi \in D$. Since $D$ is dense in $\H$, we have $U(t) = e^{it\bar{A}}$ for all $t \in \R$. $\square$

<u><b>e.g.</b></u>  Suppose we have a family of transition operators $U(t)$ on $L^{2}(\R)$, defined by $(U(t)f)(x)=f(x+t)$. Then we can apply Stone's theorem to find the infinitesimal generator: $$Af(x) = \lim_{t\to 0} \frac{U(t)f(x)-f(x)}{it} = \lim_{t\to 0} \frac{f(x+t)-f(x)}{it} = -i\frac{\d f}{\d x}(x),$$so $A=-i\frac{\d}{\d x}$ the infinitesimal generator of the unitary group $U(t)$.
