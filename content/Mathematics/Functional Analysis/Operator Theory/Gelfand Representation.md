The main goal is to represent a commutative Banach algebra $A$ as an algebra of continuous functions on a topological space. Specifically, we want to find a locally compact Hausdorff space $X$ such that $A$ is isomorphic to $C_0(X)$, the algebra of continuous functions on $X$ vanishing at infinity.

## Topological Interlude: The Weak* Topology

> [!definition] Weak\* Topology
> Let $X$ be a Banach space and $X^*$ its [[Mathematics/Linear Algebra/Duality#^6c7627|dual space]] of all bounded linear functionals on $X$.
> - We say a net $\{\varphi_{\alpha}\} \subset X^{*}$ converges in the weak\* topology to $\varphi \in X^{*}$ if $\varphi_{\alpha}(x) \to \varphi(x)$ for all $x \in X$.
> - The weak\* topology is the topology generated by the subbase of open sets of the form $\{ \varphi \in X^* : |\varphi(x) - \psi(x)| < \varepsilon \}$ for $x \in X$, $\psi \in X^*$, and $\varepsilon > 0$. Any open set can be written as an intersection of finitely many such sets.

> [!theorem]
> The weak* topology is [[Hausdorff Space#^f7bcc8|Hausdorff]].

*Proof* This is a corollary of the Hahn-Banach theorem.

> [!theorem] Banach-Alaoglu Theorem
> Let $X$ be a Banach space. Then the closed unit ball in $X^*$ (with respect to the norm of $X^*$) is weak*-compact.

## The Character Space

> [!definition] Character
> Let $A$ be a commutative [[Normed and Banach Algebras#^040470|Banach algebra]]. A *character* on $A$ is a non-zero algebra homomorphism $\tau\colon A \to \newcommand{\C}{\mathbb{C}}\C$; that is, a non-zero linear functional such that $\tau(ab) = \tau(a)\tau(b)$ for all $a, b \in A$.
> The set of all characters on $A$ is denoted by $\Omega(A)$ and is called the *character space* or *spectrum* of $A$.

<u><b>e.g.</b></u>  Let $X$ be a compact Hausdorff space and let $A = C(X)$. For any fixed $x \in X$, the evaluation map $\varepsilon_{x}\colon C(X) \to \C$ defined by $\varepsilon_x(f) = f(x)$ is a character.

> [!remark]+ Character Space is boring for non-commutative algebras
> Characters can be defined on any Banach algebra $A$. However, for non-commutative algebras, the character space may not be very interesting. For example, if $A = M_n(\C)$ for $n \ge 2$, then $\Omega(A)$ is empty. Let us consider the $n=2$ case. For the sake of contradiction, assume $\tau\colon A\to \C$ is a character of $A$, then we have $$\tau\left(\begin{pmatrix}1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}\right)\cdot\tau\left(\begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}\right)=\tau\left(\begin{pmatrix}1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}\right)=\tau\left(\begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}\right).$$At the same time, $$\tau\left(\begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}\right)\cdot\tau\left(\begin{pmatrix}1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}\right)=\tau\left(\begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}\right)=\tau\left(\begin{pmatrix}0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}\right)=0.$$This shows that $\tau\left(\begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}\right)=0$. Similarly, we can show that all of the $$\tau\left(\begin{pmatrix}1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}\right),\tau\left(\begin{pmatrix}0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0\end{pmatrix}\right), \tau\left(\begin{pmatrix}0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}\right)$$are zeroes as well. Therefore $\tau$ is the zero map, yielding a contradiction.

> [!lemma]
> Any character $\tau$ on a Banach algebra $A$ is bounded. More precisely, $\|\tau\| = 1$. If $A$ is unital, then $\tau(1)=1$. ^4f91d7

Since $\Omega(A)$ is a subset of the dual space $A^*$, we can equip $\Omega(A)$ with the weak* topology.

> [!theorem] 
> The character space $\Omega(A)$ of a commutative Banach algebra is a locally compact Hausdorff space when equipped with the weak* topology. If $A$ is unital, then $\Omega(A)$ is compact.

*Proof*  $\Omega(A)$ is Hausdorff because the weak* topology is Hausdorff. By [[Mathematics/Functional Analysis/Operator Theory/Gelfand Representation#^4f91d7|the lemma]], any character $\tau$ has $\|\tau\|=1$, so $\Omega(A)$ is a subset of the closed unit ball in $A^*$. By the Banach-Alaoglu theorem, this unit ball is weak*-compact.
Case 1: $A$ is a unital algebra. We will show that $\Omega(A)$ is a weak*-closed subset of the unit ball (ref. [[Compactness of Topological Space#^f5bb06|theorem]]). For any $a,b \in A$, define the map $F_{a,b}\colon A^* \to \C$ by $F_{a,b}(\varphi) = \varphi(ab) - \varphi(a)\varphi(b)$, and $G\colon A^{*} \to \C$ by $G(\varphi) = \varphi(1)$. The maps $F_{a,b}$ and $G$ are continuous in the weak* topology. A functional $\tau \in A^*$ is a character if and only if $F_{a,b}(\tau)=0$ for all $a,b \in A$ and $\tau(1)=1$ (which implies $\tau \neq 0$). Therefore, we can write $\Omega(A)$ as:  $$ \Omega(A) = \left( \bigcap_{a,b \in A} F_{a,b}^{-1}(\{0\}) \right) \cap G^{-1}(\{1\}) $$Since $F_{a,b}$ and $G$ are weak*-continuous, the sets $F_{a,b}^{-1}(\{0\})$ and $G^{-1}(\{1\})$ are weak*-closed. The intersection of closed sets is closed. Thus, $\Omega(A)$ is a weak*-closed subset of the compact unit ball, and is therefore compact.
Case 2: $A$ is any commutative Banach algebra. We claim that the set $K = \Omega(A) \cup \{0\}$ is weak*-compact. A functional $\varphi \in A^*$ is in $K$ if and only if it is a homomorphism (we allow the zero homomorphism here). $$ K = \{ \varphi \in A^* \mid F_{a,b}(\varphi) = 0, \forall a,b \in A \} = \bigcap_{a,b \in A} F_{a,b}^{-1}(\{0\}) \text{} $$As before, this shows $K$ is a weak*-closed subset of the unit ball (since any homomorphism $\varphi \in A^*$ has $\|\varphi\| \le 1$), so $K$ is weak*-compact. The character space $\Omega(A)$ is $K \setminus \{0\}$. Since $K$ is a compact Hausdorff space, and $\{0\}$ is a closed point, $\Omega(A)$ is an open subset of $K$. An open subset of a compact Hausdorff space is locally compact. Hence, $\Omega(A)$ is locally compact. $\square$

There is a deep connection between characters and maximal ideals in a commutative Banach algebra.

> [!lemma] 3
> Let $A$ be a unital commutative Banach algebra.
> 1. If $\tau \in \Omega(A)$, then $\ker(\tau)$ is a maximal ideal in $A$.
> 2. (Gelfand-Mazur, part of) If $I$ is a maximal ideal in $A$, then the quotient algebra $A/I$ is isometrically isomorphic to $\C$ via the map $\theta: \C \to A/I$ where $\lambda \mapsto \lambda \cdot 1 + I$.

*Proof of (i)*
Let $\tau \in \Omega(A)$. Since $\tau$ is a homomorphism, its kernel $I = \ker(\tau)$ is an ideal. Since $\tau \neq 0$, $I$ is a proper ideal. Suppose $J$ is an ideal such that $I \subsetneq J$. Let $a \in J \setminus I$. Then $\tau(a) \neq 0$.
Consider the element $b = \tau(a)^{-1}a$, which is in $J$. We have $\tau(b) = \tau(a)^{-1}\tau(a) = 1$. Since $A$ is unital, $\tau(1)=1$, so $\tau(1-b) = \tau(1)-\tau(b) = 0$. This means $1-b \in \ker(\tau) = I \subset J$. Since both $b$ and $1-b$ are in $J$, their sum $1 = b + (1-b)$ must also be in $J$. Thus, $J=A$, which proves that $I$ is a maximal ideal. $\square$

> [!theorem] 
> Let $A$ be a unital commutative Banach algebra. The mapping $\tau \mapsto \ker(\tau)$ is a bijection from $\Omega(A)$ onto the set of all maximal ideals of $A$. In particular, since every unital commutative Banach algebra has a maximal ideal, $\Omega(A) \neq \emptyset$.

*Proof*
By Lemma 3(i), the map is well-defined.

* **Injectivity:** Let $\tau_1, \tau_2 \in \Omega(A)$ with $\ker(\tau_1) = \ker(\tau_2)$. For any $a \in A$, the element $a - \tau_2(a) \cdot 1$ has $\tau_2(a - \tau_2(a) \cdot 1) = \tau_2(a) - \tau_2(a)\tau_2(1) = 0$. So, $a - \tau_2(a) \cdot 1 \in \ker(\tau_2) = \ker(\tau_1)$. Applying $\tau_1$ gives $\tau_1(a - \tau_2(a) \cdot 1) = 0$, which means $\tau_1(a) - \tau_2(a)\tau_1(1) = 0$. Since $\tau_1(1)=1$, we get $\tau_1(a) = \tau_2(a)$. As this holds for all $a \in A$, $\tau_1 = \tau_2$.
* **Surjectivity:** Let $M$ be a maximal ideal in $A$. Let $q: A \to A/M$ be the quotient homomorphism, with $\ker(q)=M$. By Lemma 3(ii) (Gelfand-Mazur), there is an isomorphism $\theta: \C \to A/M$. We can define $\tau = \theta^{-1} \circ q : A \to \C$. This $\tau$ is a homomorphism, and its kernel is $\ker(q) = M$. Since $M$ is a proper ideal, $\tau$ is not the zero homomorphism. Thus, $\tau \in \Omega(A)$ and $\ker(\tau) = M$. $\square$

<u><b>e.g.</b></u> For $A=C(X)$ where $X$ is a compact Hausdorff space, we saw that the evaluation maps $\varepsilon_x$ are characters. The kernel of $\varepsilon_x$ is $M_x = \{f \in C(X) \mid f(x) = 0\}$, which is a maximal ideal. One can prove that *every* maximal ideal of $C(X)$ is of the form $M_x$ for some unique $x \in X$. This establishes a bijection between $X$ and the set of maximal ideals. The map $x \mapsto \varepsilon_x$ is a homeomorphism from $X$ onto $\Omega(C(X))$.

> [!lemma] 5
> Let $A$ be a unital commutative Banach algebra and let $a \in A$. The following are equivalent:
> 1. $a \in \operatorname{Inv}(A)$ (i.e., $a$ is invertible).
> 2. $a$ does not belong to any maximal ideal of $A$.
> 3. $\tau(a) \neq 0$ for all $\tau \in \Omega(A)$.

## The Gelfand Transform

> [!definition] Gelfand Transform
> Let $A$ be a commutative [[Normed and Banach Algebras#^040470|Banach algebra]]. The *Gelfand transform* is the map $\Gamma\colon A \to C_0(\Omega(A))$ defined by
> $$ \Gamma(a) = \hat{a} $$
> where $\hat{a}\colon \Omega(A) \to \C$ is the function given by $\hat{a}(\tau) = \tau(a)$ for all $\tau \in \Omega(A)$. The set $\hat{A} = \{\hat{a} \mid a \in A\}$ is the image of the Gelfand transform.

The function $\hat{a}$ is continuous on $\Omega(A)$ because the topology on $\Omega(A)$ is the weak* topology, which is precisely the weakest topology making all such evaluation maps $\hat{a}$ continuous. If $A$ is non-unital, one can show $\hat{a}$ vanishes at infinity, so $\hat{a} \in C_0(\Omega(A))$. If $A$ is unital, $\Omega(A)$ is compact, so $C_0(\Omega(A)) = C(\Omega(A))$.

> [!theorem] The Spectrum Formula
> Let $A$ be a unital commutative Banach algebra. For any $a \in A$, the spectrum of $a$ is precisely the range of its Gelfand transform:
> $$ \sigma(a) = \{ \hat{a}(\tau) \mid \tau \in \Omega(A) \} $$
> If $A$ is non-unital, the formula is $\sigma(a) = \{ \hat{a}(\tau) \mid \tau \in \Omega(A) \} \cup \{0\}$.

*Proof (Unital Case)*
$\lambda \in \sigma(a) \iff a - \lambda \cdot 1$ is not invertible.
By Lemma 5, this is equivalent to there existing a character $\tau \in \Omega(A)$ such that $\tau(a - \lambda \cdot 1) = 0$.
This means $\tau(a) - \lambda \tau(1) = 0$, and since $\tau(1)=1$, we get $\tau(a) = \lambda$.
This is equivalent to saying $\lambda$ is in the range of $\hat{a}$. $\square$

> [!theorem] Gelfand Representation Theorem
> For a commutative Banach algebra $A$, the Gelfand transform $\Gamma: a \mapsto \hat{a}$ is a norm-decreasing algebra homomorphism from $A$ to $C_0(\Omega(A))$.
> - The kernel of the Gelfand transform is the Jacobson radical of $A$, $\operatorname{rad}(A) = \bigcap \{ M \mid M \text{ is a maximal ideal of } A \}$.
> - The spectral radius of $a$ is given by the supremum norm of its transform: $r(a) = \|\hat{a}\|_\infty$.
> $\quad$
