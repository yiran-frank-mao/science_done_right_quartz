## Toric Actions

Among all kinds of symplectic reductions, the most well-studied are those that arise from toric symplectic manifolds. These manifolds are equipped with a Hamiltonian action of a torus. We shall study this before we proceed to the general case of symplectic reduction.

> [!definition] Toric Symplectic Manifold
> A toric symplectic manifold is a is a [[Symplectic Structures#^b558c2|symplectic manifold]] $(M,ω)$ of dimension $2n$ equipped with an [[Group Actions#^f7c6a0|effective]] [[Symplectic Reduction#^d935f6|Hamiltonian action]] of a torus $T^{n}$, and with a choice of a corresponding moment map $\mu$. ^4025d9

<u><b>e.g.</b></u>  
- Consider $T^{n}$ acting on $\newcommand{\C}{\mathbb{C}}\C^{n}$ by coordinate-wise multiplication, i.e. $T^{n}=\{(\theta_{1},\dots,\theta_{n})\mid \theta_{j}\in S^{1}\}$ acts on $\C^{n}$ by $$\newcommand{\Z}{\mathbb{Z}}(\theta_{1},\dots,\theta_{n}) * (z_{1},\dots,z_{n})=(e^{ik_{1}\theta_{1}}z_{1},\dots,e^{ik_{n}\theta_{n}}z_{n}),\quad\text{for fixed nonzero }k_{j}\in \Z.$$The moment map $\newcommand{\R}{\mathbb{R}}\mu\colon \C^{n}\to \R^{n}$ is then given by $$\mu(z_{1},\dots,z_{n})=\frac{1}{2}\left(k_{1}|z_{1}|^{2} ,\dots,k_{n}|z_{n}|^{2} \right)$$up to a constant. We shall check this is a valid effective Hamiltonian action. It is clearly effective, only the identity $(0,\dots,0)$ acts trivially. By [[The Exponential Map#^4fdba1|the example]], we know the [[The Exponential Map#^5f72f5|fundamental vector field]] generated by a basis vector $\xi_{l}$ of the Lie algebra $\newcommand{\t}{\mathfrak{t}}\t$ is given by $X^{\xi_{l}}=ik_{l}z_{l}$. Thus the corresponding Hamilton's equations are given by $$\newcommand{\dd}{\:\mathrm{d}}\newcommand{\d}{\mathrm{d}}\newcommand{\ddf}[2]{\frac{\d#1}{\d#2}}\newcommand{\pddf}[2]{\frac{\partial#1}{\partial#2}}\pddf{\mu_{l}}{x_{l}}=k_{l}x_{l},\quad -\pddf{\mu_{l}}{y_{l}}=-k_{l}y_{l}.$$It follows that $\mu_{l}=\frac{1}{2}k_{l}(x_{l}^2+y_{l}^{2})=\frac{1}{2}k_{l}|z_{l}|^{2}$ up to a constant, which is the moment map we defined above.
$\quad$
 
> [!definition] Moment Polytope
> The moment polytope of a toric symplectic manifold is its image under the moment map.

<u><b>e.g.</b></u>
- The circle $S^{1}$ acts on $S^{2}$ by rotations: $t*(\theta,\phi)=(\theta,\phi+t)$ with moment map $\mu=\cos\phi$ equal to the height function and moment polytope $[−1,1]$.
$\quad$

> [!theorem] Atiyah-Guillemin-Sternberg Convexity Theorem
> Let $(M,\omega)$ be a [[Compactness of Topological Space#^da2511|compact]] [[Connectedness and Paths#^946cc4|connected]] symplectic manifold, and let $T^m$ be an $m$-torus. Suppose that $\psi \colon T^m \to \text{Sympl}(M,\omega)$ is a hamiltonian action with moment map $\mu \colon M \to \mathbb{R}^m$. Then:
>- the levels of $\mu$ are connected;
>- the moment polytope is [[Convex Sets#^3a9d82|convex]];
>- the moment polytope is the [[Convex Sets#^4f117e|convex hull]] of the images of the fixed points of the action.
>$\quad$

> [!definition] Equivalence of Symplectic Toric Manifolds
> Two symplectic toric manifolds, $(M_{1},\omega_{1},T_{1},\mu_{1})$ and $(M_{2},\omega_{2},T_{2},\mu_{2})$ are equivalent if there exists an isomorphism $\psi\colon T_{1}\to T_{2}$ and a $\psi$-equivariant symplectomorphism $\varphi\colon M_{1}\to M_{2}$ such that $\mu_{1}=\mu_{2}\circ \varphi$.

## Delzant’s Theorem

> [!definition] Delzant Polytope
> A Delzant polytope is a [[Convex Sets#^5986ce|convex polytope]] in $\R^{n}$ satisfying the following conditions:
> - Simple: each vertex is the intersection of exactly $n$ edges.
> - Rational: each edge meeting at the vertex $p$ is of the form $p+tu_{i}$, for $t\geq 0$, and fixed $u_{i} \in \Z^{n}$;
> - Smooth: for each vertex, the corresponding vectors $u_{1},\dots,u_{n}$ can be chosen to be a basis of $\Z^{n}$.
> $\quad$

> [!theorem] Delzant’s Theorem
> There is a one-to-one correspondence between the equivalence classes of toric symplectic manifolds and Delzant polytopes. Specifically, this bijective correspondence is given by the moment map. ^f18435

<u><b>e.g.</b></u>  <img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/yiran-frank-mao/image_repo/master/Obsidian/Delzant’s_theorem.svg" alt="Delzant’s_theorem" style="width:60%;"/>

> [!proposition] 
> Let $\Delta$ be an $n$-dimensional Delzant polytope, and let $(M_{\Delta},\omega_{\Delta},T^{n},\mu_{\Delta})$ be the associated symplectic toric manifold. Then $\mu_{\Delta}$ maps the fixed points of $T^{n}$ bijectively onto the vertices of $\Delta$. 

## Blow-Ups of Toric Symplectic Manifolds

We start with the blow-up at origin of $\C^{n}$:

> [!definition] Blow-Up of $\C^{n}$
> Let $L$ be the tautological line bundle over $\C P^{n-1}$, i.e., a complex line bundle whose fiber over each point $[p]\in\C P^{n-1}$ is precisely the line represented by that point. The blow-up of $\C^{n}$ at origin is the total space of the bundle $L$, that is, $$\{([p],z)\mid [p]\in\C P^{n-1}, z=\lambda p \text{ for some }\lambda\in \C \}.$$
> The corresponding blow-down map is $$\beta\colon L\to \C^{n},\quad ([p],z)\mapsto z.$$
> The set $E:=\{([p],0) \mid [p]\in \C P^{n-1}\}$ is called the exceptional divisor.

> [!theorem]
> Suppose $\Delta$ is an $n$-dimensional Delzant polytope, and let $(M_{\Delta}, \omega_{\Delta},T^{n},\mu_{\Delta})$ be the associated symplectic toric manifold. The $\varepsilon$-blow-up of $(M_{\Delta}, \omega_{\Delta})$ at a fixed point $q$ (i.e. $q$ is in the isotropy) is the symplectic toric manifold associated to the polytope $\Delta_{\varepsilon}$ obtained from $\Delta$ by replacing the vertex $p=\mu_{\Delta}(q)$ by the $n$ vertices
> $$p + \varepsilon u_{i}, \quad i=1, \dots, n.$$

> [!remark]+ Geometric Interpretation of Blow-Up
> In other words, the moment polytope for the blow-up of $(M_{\Delta},\omega_{\Delta})$ at $q$ is obtained from $\Delta$ by chopping off the corner corresponding to $q$, thus substituting the original set of vertices by the same set with the vertex corresponding to $q$ replaced by exactly $n$ new vertices:
> <img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/yiran-frank-mao/image_repo/master/Obsidian/symplectic_blow_up.svg" alt="symplectic_blow_up" style="width:25%;"/>
> 

<u><b>e.g.</b></u>  $\C$ is a toric symplectic manifold with the standard $S^1$ action. It corresponds to the Delzant polytope $\R_{\geq 0}$. Chopping off the vertex yields the same polytope $\R_{\geq 0}$, so the blow-up manifold remains to be $\C$. One can see this through symplectic cut as well (refer to [[Toric Symplectic Manifold#^2dd0da|the example]]).

## Symplectic Cuts

For toric symplectic manifolds, blow-ups are equivalent to symplectic cuts, which are a more general construction applicable to any symplectic manifold. We shall see this on $\C$ with the standard $S^1$ action:

<u><b>e.g.</b></u>  Let us apply the symplectic cut to $\C$ at the origin point. Let $\Phi(z,w):=\frac{1}{2}(|z|^2-|w|^2)$ be defined on $\C\times\C$. Then for some $\varepsilon>0$, we have $$\Phi^{-1}(\varepsilon)=\{(z,w)\mid |z|^2-|w|^2=2\varepsilon\}$$Let $A:=\{(z,0)\mid |z|^2=2\varepsilon\}$ and $B:=\left\{(z,w)\mid w=\sqrt{|z|^2-2\varepsilon}e^{i\theta}\right\}$, then $\Phi^{-1}(\varepsilon)=A\cup B$. Note that $A\cong S^1$, $B=\{(z,\theta)\mid |z|^2\geq 2\varepsilon\}\cong S^1 \times (\C\setminus\{0\})$, so $$\begin{aligned}M_{\geq\varepsilon}&=\Phi^{-1}(\varepsilon)/S^1=(S^1/S^1)\cup (\C\setminus\{0\}) \\&=\{0\}\cup (\C\setminus\{0\})=\C.\end{aligned}$$ ^2dd0da
