## The Fundamental Braid

> [!definition] Positive Braid
> A positive braid is the [[Braids and Braids Group#^311e98|braid]] that can be written as product of positive powers of generators. Denote the set of positive $n$-braids as $B_{n}^{+}$, clearly $B_{n}^{+}$ forms a monoid.

> [!definition] Garside's Fundamental Braid
> The Garside's fundamental braid $\Delta_{n}$ is the unique positive braid in $B_{n}^{+}$ such that any two strands cross exactly once. That is, it is the half twist of the identity braid. One representative of $\Delta_{n}$ in $B_{n}$ is $$\Delta_n = (\sigma_1 \sigma_2 \cdots \sigma_{n-1}) (\sigma_1 \sigma_2 \cdots \sigma_{n-2}) \cdots\ (\sigma_1 \sigma_2) \sigma_1 = \prod_{i=1}^{n-1}\prod_{j=1}^{n-i}\sigma_j.$$ We shall normally abbreviate $\Delta_{n}$ to the simpler form $\Delta$ once the braid group is given.

> [!proposition]  
> The following statements are equivalent:  
> 1. $\Delta_n$ is the fundamental braid in $B_n$.  
> 2. $\Delta_n$ is the unique positive braid where any two strands cross exactly once.  
> 3. $\Delta_n$ is the half twist of the identity braid of $n$ strands.
> $\quad$

*Proof*  We omit the proof of the uniqueness of a positive braid where any two strands cross exactly once. A technical proof may involve lattice structures and Garside theory.    
**$(1) \implies (2)$:** It is straightforward to see that $\Delta_n$ is a positive braid where any two strands cross exactly once by stacking generators.  
**$(2) \implies (3)$:** If $\Delta_n$ is a positive braid where any two strands cross exactly once, we determine the permutation it induces. Since strand $1$ crosses each of strands $2,3,\dots,n$ exactly once, it must end at position $n$. Similarly, strand $2$ crosses strands $1, 3, 4, \dots, n$ exactly once, meaning it crosses all strands except itself. Since it has already crossed strand $1$ (which is now at position $n$), strand $2$ must end at position $n - 1$. Continuing this reasoning, strand $i$ must end at position $n - i + 1$. This corresponds to the reverse permutation $(n \quad n-1 \cdots 2 \quad 1)$. A half-twist of the identity braid rotates the bottom endpoints by $\pi$, sending strand $i$ to position $n - i + 1$. Therefore, $\Delta_n$ induces the same permutation as a half-twist. Moreover, since each pair of strands crosses exactly once, the geometric realization of $\Delta_n$ corresponds precisely to a half-twist.  
**$(3) \implies (1)$:** If $\Delta_n$ is the half-twist of the identity braid, one can easily verify that  $$  
\Delta_n = (\sigma_1 \sigma_2 \cdots \sigma_{n-1})(\sigma_1 \sigma_2 \cdots \sigma_{n-2}) \cdots (\sigma_1 \sigma_2)(\sigma_1)  
$$  is a valid word representation. $\square$

> [!proposition]
> The fundamental braid can always be expressed in a word that begins or ends with any generator while remaining positive. That is, for any generator $\sigma_{i}$, we can find positive braids $x_{i}$ and $y_{i}$ such that $$\sigma_{i}x_{i}=\Delta=y_{i}\sigma_{i}$$ ^a92d77

> [!lemma]
> In $B_{n}$, the identity $\sigma_{i}\Delta=\Delta \sigma_{n-i}$ holds. ^bbbe47

*Proof*  One can show this algebraically using the braid word expression of $\Delta_n$, by inductively proving that $\sigma_1\Delta_k = \Delta_k\sigma_{k}$ for all $1\leq k\leq n$. Here, we present a more intuitive geometric proof using the half-twist definition.
A key property of twisting is that its application is independent of location. Thus, for $\sigma_i \Delta_n$, we can apply the inverse half-twist—fixing the top endpoints and $\sigma_i$, while rotating the bottom endpoints by $-\pi$—which results in $\sigma_i$. Similarly, for $\Delta_n \sigma_{n-i}$, we fix $\Delta_n$ on top and rotate the bottom $\sigma_i$ by $-\pi$. This flips the crossing of the $(n-i)$th and $(n-i+1)$th strands to the left, ultimately yielding $\Delta_n \Delta_n^{-1} \sigma_i$. Consequently, $\sigma_i \Delta_n$ and $\Delta_n \sigma_{n-i}$ are identical after applying the inverse half-twist, implying they were originally equal.
A similar equation holds for $\Delta_n^{-1}$, but in this case, we apply a half-twist instead. $\square$

> [!corollary]
> $\Delta^{2}$ commutes with every generator. In fact, the centre of $B_{n}$ is cyclic and generated by $\Delta^{2}$.

*Proof*  It suffices to show that it commutes with all generators. For any positive integer $i\leq n$, we have $$ \Delta_n^2 \sigma_i = \Delta_n (\Delta_n \sigma_i) = \Delta_n \sigma_{n-i} \Delta_n = \sigma_i \Delta_{n}^{2} $$

## Reflection

> [!definition] Reflection
> Define the automorphism of $B_{n}$ conjugating elements with $\Delta$ as the reflection $\tau$. That is $$\tau\colon B_{n} \to B_{n},\quad \beta\mapsto \Delta \beta\Delta^{-1}$$ Clearly it maps any generator $\sigma_{i}$ to $\sigma_{n-i}$.

> [!proposition]
> $\tau^{2}$ is the identity automorphism.

*Proof*  $$\tau^{2}(\beta)=\Delta\Delta\beta\Delta^{-1}\Delta^{-1}=\beta$$The last equality follows from the fact that $\Delta^{2}$ commutes with every generator. $\square$

## Prefix

> [!definition] Prefix
> Let $a,b\in B_{n}^{+}$. We define $a\leq b$ if there exists $c\in B_{n}^{+}$ such that $b=ac$, said $a$ is a prefix of $b$.

> [!proposition]
> The prefix relation $\leq$ is a [[Order#^fa7f22|partial order]] on $B_{n}^{+}$, invariant under left multiplication.

*Proof*  

> [!proposition] Greatest Common Prefix
> For any two elements $a,b\in B_{n}^{+}$ there exists a unique greatest common prefix $a\wedge b$, that is $a\wedge b \leq a$, $a \wedge b\leq b$, and if $c\leq a$ and $c\leq b$ then $c\leq a\wedge b$.

## Garside Normal Form

> [!definition] Permutation Braids
> The positive prefixes of $\Delta$ are called permutation braids.

<u><b>e.g.</b></u>  Consider $B_{4}$, $\Delta=\sigma_{3}\sigma_{2}\sigma_{1}\sigma_{2}\sigma_{1}\sigma_{1}$, two of the permutation braids are $\sigma_{3}$ and $\sigma_{3}\sigma_{2}\sigma_{1}\sigma_{2}$:

![permutation_braids_1.svg|120](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/yiran-frank-mao/image_repo/master/Obsidian/permutation_braids_1.svg)
![permutation_braids_2.svg|120](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/yiran-frank-mao/image_repo/master/Obsidian/permutation_braids_2.svg)

We can see that $\alpha(\sigma_{3})=\begin{pmatrix}1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}3 & 4\end{pmatrix}$ and $\alpha(\sigma_{3}\sigma_{2}\sigma_{1}\sigma_{2})=\begin{pmatrix}1 & 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}2\end{pmatrix}$.

By the crossing number definition of the fundamental braid, each prefix corresponds to a braid in which each pair of strands crosses at most once. In fact, we have the following proposition:

> [!proposition]  
> The following statements are equivalent for a positive braid $\beta$:  
> - $\beta$ is a permutation braid.  
> - Each pair of strands in $\beta$ crosses at most once.  
> - $\beta \in B_n/\langle \sigma_1^2, \sigma_2^2, \dots, \sigma_{n-1}^2 \rangle$.
>$\quad$

If $\beta$ is a positive braid where no pair of strands crosses more than once, it induces a unique permutation $\varphi(\beta)$ in the symmetric group $S_n$, where $\varphi$ is the isomorphism $B_n/\langle \sigma_{1}^{2}, \sigma_{2}^{2}, \dots, \sigma_{n-1}^{2} \rangle\cong S_{n}$. Conversely, every permutation can be realized by such a braid.  Thus, any positive permutation braid is uniquely identified by a permutation in $S_n$. These permutation braids serve as the fundamental building blocks of the normal form.
Suppose $\beta \in B_n^+$ is a positive braid. We can attempt to factor it into a product of permutation braids using a greedy approach: first, select the longest possible prefix of $\beta$ that forms a valid permutation braid by ensuring that each pair of strands crosses at most once. Then, continue this process with the remaining part of $\beta$.

<u><b>e.g.</b></u>  In $B_4$, let $\beta = \sigma_3\sigma_1\sigma_2\sigma_1\sigma_2\sigma_3$. Clearly, $\sigma_3$ is a permutation braid. Extending further, $\sigma_3\sigma_1\sigma_2\sigma_1$ remains a valid permutation braid, as each pair of strands crosses at most once. However, adding $\sigma_2$ to this prefix results in $\sigma_3\sigma_1\sigma_2\sigma_1\sigma_2$, where the first and second strands cross a second time, violating the condition (see below diagram). Thus, we factor out $p_1 = \sigma_3\sigma_1\sigma_2\sigma_1$, leaving the remainder $p_2 = \sigma_2\sigma_3$, which can be similarly verified as a permutation braid.


<img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/yiran-frank-mao/image_repo/master/Obsidian/positive_braid_factorization_eg.svg" style="width:25%;"/>

To ensure uniqueness in this factorization, at each step, we extract the longest possible permutation braid, meaning that any longer prefix would fail to be a permutation braid. To formalize this property, we define such a product $p_1 p_2$ as *left-weighted*:

> [!definition] Left-Weighted
> A product of permutation braids $p_{1}p_{2}$ is called left-weighted if $(p_{1}p_{2})\wedge \Delta=p_{1}$.

Now we can express any positive braid as a product of permutation braids. To extend this unique factorization to all braids, we require an algorithm that transforms any braid containing negative generators into a positive braid.

Since the fundamental braid can always be rewritten to end with any generator while remaining positive, each inverse generator $\sigma_i^{-1}$ in a braid $\beta \in B_n$ can be rewritten as $\Delta_n^{-1} \Delta_n \sigma_i$. Here, $\Delta_n \sigma_i$ is a positive braid, where the last generator of $\Delta_n$ cancels with $\sigma_i$. 

By the [[Garside Normal Form#^bbbe47|lemma]] all occurrences of $\Delta_n^{-1}$ can be pulled to the front by flipping the indices of the generators preceding it. Consequently, we can express $\beta$ in the form $\beta = \Delta_n^r \beta^+$, where $\beta^+$ is a positive braid. Applying the earlier factorization technique to $\beta^+$, we obtain the Garside normal form:

> [!theorem] Garside Left Normal Form
> Every braid $\beta$ can be represented uniquely as a braid word $$\Delta^{r}p_{1}p_{2}\dots p_{k}$$where $\newcommand{\Z}{\mathbb{Z}}r\in\Z_{\leq 0}$, each $p_{i}$ is a permutation braid, and $p_{i}p_{i+1}$ is left-weighted. And we call $k$ the canonical length of $\beta$ and the integer $r$ the infimum of $\beta$.

*Proof*  Whereas it is easy and intuitive to describe this form in $B_n$, proving its uniqueness rigorously requires significantly more work. For detailed proofs, one can refer to [[Garside Normal Form#^fbc3de|Garside's original paper]]. $\square$

<u><b>e.g.</b></u>  In $B_3$, the fundamental braid is given by $\Delta_3 = \sigma_1 \sigma_2 \sigma_1 = \sigma_2 \sigma_1 \sigma_2$. Consider the braid $\beta = \sigma_1\sigma_2^{-1}$. We can factor it step by step into its Garside normal form as follows: $$\begin{aligned}\beta &= \sigma_1\sigma_2^{-1} \\&= \sigma_1 \Delta_3^{-1} \Delta_3 \sigma_2^{-1} \\&= \sigma_1 \Delta_3^{-1} (\sigma_2\sigma_1\sigma_2) \sigma_2^{-1} \\&= \sigma_1 \Delta_3^{-1} \sigma_2\sigma_1 \\&= \Delta_3^{-1} \sigma_{2} \sigma_2\sigma_1 \\&= \Delta_3^{-1} (\sigma_{2})(\sigma_2\sigma_1).\end{aligned}$$Here, $p_1 = \sigma_2$ and $p_2 = \sigma_2\sigma_1$ are permutation braids. Thus, we conclude that $\inf(\beta) = -1$ and the canonical length of $\beta$ is $2$.
