## Singular Homology

Recall that a $\Delta$-complex is built out of simplices, and $C_{n}^\Delta(X)=\Z\{n\text{-simplices of }X\}$. Singular homology generalizes this by considering all continuous maps from the standard simplex into the space, not just those that are part of a specific simplicial decomposition.

> [!definition] Singular Chain Complex
> For a topological space $X$, the *singular chain complex* is a sequence of abelian groups $C_n(X)$ and homomorphisms $d_n$, called boundary maps:
> $$ \dots \xrightarrow{d_{n+2}} C_{n+1}(X) \xrightarrow{d_{n+1}} C_{n}(X) \xrightarrow{d_n} C_{n-1}(X) \xrightarrow{d_{n-1}} \dots \xrightarrow{d_1} C_0(X) \xrightarrow{d_{0}} 0. $$
> - *Singular $n$-chains* $C_n(X)$ is the free abelian group generated by all continuous maps $\sigma\colon \Delta^{n} \to X$, where $\Delta^n$ is the standard n-simplex. $$ C_n(X) = \mathbb{Z}\{\sigma\colon \Delta^n \to X\} $$
> - The *boundary map* $d_{n}\colon C_{n}(X) \to C_{n-1}(X)$ is defined on a generator $\sigma$ by: $$ d_n(\sigma) = \sum_{i=0}^n (-1)^i \sigma|_{[v_0, \dots, \hat{v}_i, \dots, v_n]}, $$ where $\sigma|_{[v_0, \dots, \hat{v}_i, \dots, v_n]}$ is the restriction of $\sigma$ to the $i$-th face of $\Delta^{n}$.
> 
> A key property is that $d_n \circ d_{n+1} = 0$.

> [!definition] Singular Homology
> An element of $\ker(d_{n})\subset C_{n}(X)$ is called an *$n$-cycle*, and an element of $\text{im}(d_{n+1})$ is called an *$n$-boundary*.
> The $n$-th *singular homology group* of $X$ is the [[Quotients#^6d4873|quotient group]]:
> $$ H_n(X) = \frac{\ker(d_n)}{\text{im}(d_{n+1})}. $$

> [!definition] Chain Map
> A *chain map* between two chain complexes $(C_*, d_*^C)$ and $(D_*, d_*^D)$ is a sequence of homomorphisms $f_n\colon C_n \to D_n$ such that the following diagram commutes:

## Homotopy Invariance

It turns out that if two topological spaces are homotopic, then their homologies are isomorphic. To show this, we need the notion of a chain homotopy:

> [!definition] Chain Homotopy
> Let $f, g \colon C_* \to D_*$ be two [[Singular Homology#^ff17c9|chain maps]]. A *chain homotopy* between $f$ and $g$ is a sequence of homomorphisms $P_n\colon C_n \to D_{n+1}$ such that for all $n$:
> $$ d_{n+1}^D \circ P_n + P_{n-1} \circ d_n^C = g_n - f_n. $$

> [!proposition]
> A homotopy $F\colon X\times I \to Y$ from $f$ to $g$ induces a chain homotopy $P\colon C_{*}(X) \to C_{*}(Y)$ from $f^\#$ to $g^\#$.
> 

*Proof Sketch*  Given a singular $n$-simplex $\sigma\colon \Delta^n \to X$, we can define a map $\tilde{\sigma}\colon \Delta^n \times I \to Y$ by $\tilde{\sigma}(x, t) = F(\sigma(x), t)$. The "prism" $\Delta^n \times I$ can be subdivided into $n+1$ simplices of dimension $n+1$.
Let the vertices of $\Delta^n \times \{0\}$ be $v_0, \dots, v_n$ and the vertices of $\Delta^n \times \{1\}$ be $w_0, \dots, w_n$. The prism is the union of $(n+1)$-simplices $[v_0, \dots, v_i, w_i, \dots, w_n]$ for $i=0, \dots, n$.
The chain homotopy map $P_n: C_n(X) \to C_{n+1}(Y)$ is defined on a generator $\sigma$ as: $$ P_n(\sigma) = \sum_{i=0}^n (-1)^i \tilde{\sigma}|_{[v_0, \dots, v_i, w_i, \dots, w_n]}. $$A direct (but lengthy) calculation shows that this definition of $P_n$ satisfies the chain homotopy equation $d P + P d = g^\# - f^\#$. The calculation involves carefully tracking how the boundary of the prism $d(\Delta^n \times I)$ relates to the faces of the individual $(n+1)$-simplices in the subdivision. The "top" face $\Delta^n \times \{1\}$ corresponds to $g_\#(\sigma)$, the "bottom" face $\Delta^n \times \{0\}$ corresponds to $f_\#(\sigma)$, and the "side" faces $d\Delta^n \times I$ correspond to $P(d\sigma)$. $\square$

> [!theorem] Homotopy Invariance
> If two maps $f, g\colon X \to Y$ are [[Homotopy Types#^2c10b4|homotopic]], then they induce the same homomorphism on homology groups:
> $$ f_* = g_*: H_n(X) \to H_n(Y) \quad \forall n \ge 0. $$

*Proof*  Suppose $f$ and $g$ are homotopic, and $F\colon X\times I \to Y$ is the homotopy. By the previous proposition, the homotopy $F$ induces a chain homotopy $P$ from $f^{\#}$ to $g^{\#}$ so that $d_{n+1}^Y \circ P_n + P_{n-1} \circ d_n^X = g_{n}^{\#} - f_{n}^{\#}.$
For any homology class $[\alpha] \in H_n(X)$ represented by a cycle $\alpha \in \ker(d_n^X)$, we have: $$\left[ g_{n}^{\#}\circ \alpha - f_{n}^{\#}\circ \alpha\right]=\left[d_{n+1}^{Y} \circ P_{n} \circ\alpha+ P_{n-1} \circ d_n^{X}\circ\alpha\right]=0\in H_{n}(Y).$$Therefore, $[g_{n}^{\#}\circ\alpha]=[f_{n}^{\#}\circ \alpha]$, which means $g_{*}([\alpha])=f_{*}([\alpha])$, we conclude that $f_* = g_*$. $\square$

> [!corollary]
> If $X$ and $Y$ are homotopy equivalent, then their homology groups are isomorphic:
> $$ H_n(X) \cong H_n(Y) \quad \forall n \ge 0. $$

## Homology of a Point

A simple but important calculation is the homology of a single point space, denoted $\{*\}$.

> [!lemma] Homology of a Point
> The homology groups of a point are:
> $$ H_n(\{*\}) = \begin{cases} \mathbb{Z} & n = 0, \\ 0 & n > 0. \end{cases} $$ ^f1d145

*Proof*  For any $n \ge 0$, there is a unique continuous map $\sigma_n\colon \Delta^n \to \{*\}$. Thus, $C_n(\{*\}) \cong \mathbb{Z}$, generated by $\sigma_n$. The boundary map $d_n\colon C_n(\{*\}) \to C_{n-1}(\{*\})$ sends $\sigma_n$ to: $$ d_n(\sigma_n) = \sum_{i=0}^n (-1)^i \sigma_{n-1} = \begin{cases} \sigma_{n-1} & \text{if } n \text{ is even and } n>0, \\ 0 & \text{if } n \text{ is odd}. \end{cases} $$The resulting chain complex is: $$ \dots \xrightarrow{\cong} \mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{0} \mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{\cong} \mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{0} C_0(\{*\}) \cong \mathbb{Z} \to 0 $$The homology of this complex is straightforward to compute. For $n>0$, the kernel of one map is the image of the previous one, so $H_n(\{pt\}) = 0$. For $n=0$, $\ker(d_0) = C_0 \cong \mathbb{Z}$ and $\text{im}(d_1) = 0$, so $H_0(\{*\}) \cong \mathbb{Z}$. $\square$
