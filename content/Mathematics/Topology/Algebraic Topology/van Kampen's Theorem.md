## The Seifert-van Kampen Theorem

> [!theorem] van Kampen Theorem
> Let $X = \bigcup_{\alpha} A_{\alpha}$ be a [[Topological Spaces#^65c94a|topological space]], with $x_0 \in \bigcap_{\alpha} A_{\alpha}$. Suppose each $A_{\alpha}$ is open and [[Connectedness and Paths#^630354|path-connected]].
> 1.  If each intersection $A_{\alpha} \cap A_{\beta}$ is path-connected for all $\alpha, \beta$, then the homomorphism $\Phi: \underset{\alpha}{*} \pi_1(A_\alpha, x_0) \to \pi_1(X, x_0)$ induced by the inclusion maps $i_\alpha: A_\alpha \hookrightarrow X$ is surjective.
> 2.  If, additionally, each triple intersection $A_{\alpha} \cap A_{\beta} \cap A_{\gamma}$ is path-connected for all $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$, then the kernel of $\Phi$ is the normal subgroup $N$ generated by all elements of the form $i_{\alpha\beta*}(\omega) (i_{\beta\alpha*}(\omega))^{-1}$ for $\omega \in \pi_1(A_\alpha \cap A_\beta, x_0)$.
>
> In this case, $\Phi$ induces an isomorphism:
> $$ \pi_1(X, x_0) \cong \left(\underset{\alpha}{*} \pi_1(A_\alpha, x_0)\right) / N. $$ ^9e0d9b

The path-connectedness of the intersections is a crucial hypothesis. If it fails, the theorem does not apply and can give incorrect results:

<u><b>e.g.</b></u>  Cover the circle $S^1$ with two open arcs $A = S^1 \setminus \{p\}$ and $B = S^1 \setminus \{q\}$, where $p \neq q$.
* We have $\pi_1(A, x_0) = 0$ and $\pi_1(B, x_0) = 0$ since $A$ and $B$ are contractible.
* A naive application of the theorem might suggest $\pi_1(S^1) \cong 0 * 0 = 0$, which is false.
* The issue is that the intersection $A \cap B$ consists of two disjoint arcs and is therefore not path-connected. Condition (1) of the theorem is not met.

<u><b>e.g.</b></u>  Let $X$ be a sphere with two points identified. Let $x_0$ be the identification point. Consider an open cover by three sets $A=X\setminus\{a\}$, $B=X\setminus\{b\}$, and $C=X\setminus\{c\}$ where $a,b,c$ are points on the two "lobes" and the "equator" respectively.
* Each of $A, B, C$ deformation retracts to a circle, so $A \simeq S^1, B \simeq S^1, C \simeq S^1$.
* The pairwise intersections $A \cap B$, $A \cap C$, and $B \cap C$ are contractible, thus path-connected.
* Van Kampen part (1) would suggest $\pi_1(X)$ is a quotient of $\pi_1(A) * \pi_1(B) * \pi_1(C) \cong \mathbb{Z} * \mathbb{Z} * \mathbb{Z}$.
* However, the space $X$ is the wedge sum $S^1 \lor S^1$, so its fundamental group is $\pi_1(X) \cong \mathbb{Z} * \mathbb{Z}$.
* The issue here is that the triple intersection $A \cap B \cap C$ is not path-connected, so condition (2) does not apply.

## The "Good Point" Condition for Infinite Unions

> [!definition] Wedge Sum
> The *wedge sum* of two [[Topological Spaces#^65c94a|topological spaces]] $X$ and $Y$ at points $x_{0}\in X$ and $y_{0}\in Y$ is the [[Topological Spaces#^65c94a|topological space]] $X\vee Y:= (X\sqcup Y)/x_{0}\sim y_{0}$.
> <img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/yiran-frank-mao/image_repo/master/Obsidian/wedge_sum.svg" alt="wedge_sum" style="width:25%;"/>

For the theorem to extend to infinite unions of closed sets, a local condition on the basepoint is often required.

> [!definition] Good Point
> A point $x_0 \in X$ is called *good* if $x_0$ has an open neighborhood $U$ that [[Homotopy Types#^05414e|deformation retracts]] to $x_0$.

<u><b>e.g.</b></u>  Consider the space $X = \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty C_n$, where $C_n$ is the circle in $\newcommand{\R}{\mathbb{R}}\R^2$ with radius $1/n$ centered at $(1/n, 0)$. The wedge point $x_0 = (0,0)$ where all circles meet is not a "good" point. Any open neighborhood of $x_0$ contains infinitely many of the circles and does not deformation retract to $x_0$. 
<img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/yiran-frank-mao/image_repo/master/Obsidian/Hawaiian_earring.svg" alt="Hawaiian_earring" style="width:20%;"/>

> [!proposition] Fundamental Group of a Wedge Sum
> Suppose $x_0 \in X_\alpha$ is a good point for each $\alpha$. Then the fundamental group of the wedge sum $\bigvee_\alpha X_\alpha$ is the free product of the fundamental groups of the components:
> $$ \pi_1\left(\bigvee_\alpha X_\alpha, x_0\right) \cong \underset{\alpha}{*} \pi_1(X_\alpha, x_0).$$

*Proof Sketch*  One can "fatten" each space $X_\alpha$ to an open set $A_\alpha$ in the wedge sum such that $A_\alpha$ deformation retracts to $X_\alpha$. The pairwise and triple intersections of these fattened sets will deformation retract to the wedge point $x_0$. Since $x_0$ is a good point, these intersections are path-connected. Van Kampen's theorem then applies to give the result. $\square$

## Applications: Complements of Links in $\mathbb{R}^3$

### Complement of a Single Circle
Let $Z=\R^3 \setminus S^1$. This space [[Homotopy Types#^05414e|deformation retracts]] to a 2-sphere union one of its diameter that pass through the removed $S^{1}$. There are two approaches to think about this. One way is that we can explicitly construct a retraction as follows: for any $x\in Z$, if $x$ is outside of the sphere, we map it to $x/\|x\|$; otherwise, $x$ is inside the sphere, we pick its closest point $\tilde{x}$ on the removed $S^{1}$, and travel away from $\tilde{x}$ until we reach either the diameter or the sphere.
Another way is to note that $Z=\R^{3}\setminus S^{1}\simeq\R^{3}\setminus (S^{1}\times\{*\})\simeq \R^{3}\setminus (S^{1}\times D^{2})$, which is the complement of a solid torus. One can visualize this by embedding the solid torus inside a 2-sphere, and then inflating the torus until it nearly touches the sphere’s surface. The space outside the torus then deformation retracts onto a 2-sphere together with one of its diameters passing through the “hole” of the torus.
<img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/yiran-frank-mao/image_repo/master/Obsidian/complement_of_circle.svg" alt="complement_of_circle" style="width:25%;"/>
Now let $X$ denote the deformation retract obtained from either approach. We can cover $X$ with two open sets: $$U:=\text{neighbourhood of diameter union the green arc},\quad V:=\text{neighbourhood of }S^{2}.$$Note that $U$ is homeomorphic to $S^{1}$, and $U\cap V$ is the green arc, which is contractible, so $$ \pi_1(Z) \cong \pi_{1}(X)\cong \pi_1(U) * \pi_1(V) \cong \mathbb{Z}.$$The generator is a loop that links with the removed circle $S^1$.

### Complement of Unlinked Circles
Let $X = \mathbb{R}^3 \setminus (C_1 \cup C_2)$, where $C_1$ and $C_2$ are two unlinked circles. By applying the Seifert-van Kampen theorem, one can show that the fundamental group is the free product of two copies of $\mathbb{Z}$.
$$ \pi_1(X) \cong \mathbb{Z} * \mathbb{Z} $$
This group is **non-abelian**. A loop winding around the first circle does not commute with a loop winding around the second. The space has the homotopy type of $(S^1 \lor S^2) \lor (S^1 \lor S^2)$.

### Complement of Linked Circles (Hopf Link)
Let $Y = \mathbb{R}^3 \setminus (L_1 \cup L_2)$, where $L_1$ and $L_2$ form a simple link (the Hopf link).
The fundamental group is the direct product (or sum) of two copies of $\mathbb{Z}$:
$$ \pi_1(Y) \cong \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z} $$
This group is **abelian**. The linking of the circles forces the generators of the fundamental group to commute. This space is claimed to be homotopy equivalent to $S^2 \lor (S^1 \times S^1)$, which has the correct fundamental group.
